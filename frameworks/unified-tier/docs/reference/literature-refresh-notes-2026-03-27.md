# Literature Refresh Notes

Date: 2026-03-27
Status: draft

## Purpose

Capture recent or still-relevant literature that should shape the next revision of the Unified Tier Framework and wickedness scoring.

## Core Foundations

### Rittel & Webber (1973)

`Dilemmas in a General Theory of Planning`

Why it still matters:

- canonical wicked-problem foundation
- supports non-definitive formulation, no stopping rule, and better/worse rather than true/false solution framing

Use in HUMMBL:

- keep as the conceptual anchor for Tier 4

### Levin et al. (2012)

`Overcoming the tragedy of super wicked problems`

Why it still matters:

- canonical super-wicked anchor
- supports time pressure, governance weakness, causers-as-solvers, and future discounting

Use in HUMMBL:

- keep as the conceptual anchor for Tier 5

## More Recent Operationally Useful Sources

### O’Connor & Douguet (2024)

`Working deliberat(iv)ely with(in) wicked problems: The existential, epistemological and ethical nexus of imperfect knowledge`

Source:

- *Futures* (2024)
- <https://doi.org/10.1016/j.futures.2024.103436>

Why it matters:

- foregrounds imperfect knowledge, urgency, high stakes, and deliberative evaluation
- useful for refreshing the framework away from purely static categorization

Potential HUMMBL effect:

- strengthens uncertainty and urgency as first-class scoring concerns
- supports a stronger distinction between decision support under uncertainty and textbook complexity

### Small-Wins Evaluation Framework

`A small wins framework to overcome the evaluation paradox of governing wicked problems`

Source:

- open-access PDF
- <https://openresearch.amsterdam/image/2023/11/22/a_small_wins_framework_to_overcome_the_evaluation_paradox_of_governing_wicked_pr.pdf>

Why it matters:

- directly addresses how to evaluate action in wicked domains without pretending to "solve" them in a simple target-based way

Potential HUMMBL effect:

- the framework should separate:
  - problem classification
  - intervention evaluation
- success criteria for Tier 4 and Tier 5 problems may need to emphasize trajectory, adaptation, and partial progress rather than definitive solution

### Gale et al. (2025)

`Tackling wicked problems through street-level diplomacy: the case of antimicrobial stewardship in Northern Europe`

Source:

- *Social Science & Medicine* (2025)
- <https://doi.org/10.1016/j.socscimed.2025.118629>

Why it matters:

- shows wickedness in practice through trust-building, discretionary action, and relational governance

Potential HUMMBL effect:

- supports adding practical governance and authority considerations to the scoring narrative
- suggests that implementation context matters, not only abstract problem shape

### Howlett et al. on pandemics as super-wicked problems

Source:

- <https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11077-021-09442-2>

Why it matters:

- shows super-wicked structure in contemporary crisis governance
- reinforces urgency, institutional weakness, and policy adaptation

Potential HUMMBL effect:

- supports a clearer Tier 5 emphasis on time pressure plus governance weakness

## Practical Takeaways For HUMMBL

1. Keep the five-tier structure unless empirical evidence forces a larger redesign.
2. Refresh the dimensional language so it better reflects:
   - contestation
   - uncertainty
   - interdependence
   - temporality
   - reversibility or path dependence
   - governance weakness
3. Treat evaluation of action in wicked domains as separate from classification of the domain itself.
4. Distinguish clearly between:
   - manual expert assessment
   - heuristic auto-classification
   - outcome validation

## Open Question

Should HUMMBL keep the existing 5-question manual rubric and merely rename dimensions, or should it move toward a clearer hybrid such as:

- contestation
- uncertainty
- path dependence
- adaptive pressure
- temporal urgency plus governance weakness

This is still open and should be decided alongside calibration work, not by prose preference alone.
