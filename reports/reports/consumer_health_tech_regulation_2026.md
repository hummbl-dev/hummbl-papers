## Executive Summary

Peptide-checker occupies a favorable but nuanced regulatory position. As a verification/education platform that provides information about peptide products — without diagnosing, treating, or making therapeutic claims — it falls outside FDA medical device jurisdiction and likely outside HIPAA scope. However, the platform must navigate FTC health claims enforcement, emerging state privacy laws (especially Washington's My Health My Data Act), and the inherently volatile peptide regulatory environment.

**Bottom line:** Peptide-checker can operate legally and safely with proper structural safeguards. The key risk is not what the platform *is*, but what it *says* — claims and marketing language determine regulatory exposure more than any other factor.

## 1. FDA Regulation: Medical Device vs. Consumer Information

### 1.1 The Medical Device Line

The FDA regulates "devices" intended to diagnose, treat, cure, mitigate, or prevent disease. Whether software qualifies depends on **intended use and marketing claims**, not underlying technology.

As of January 6, 2026, the FDA published revised guidance documents that significantly loosened oversight of digital health tools:

- **General Wellness Guidance (Revised 2026):** Non-invasive products targeting wellness purposes exclusively, posing minimal safety risk, and avoiding disease/diagnostic/clinical claims qualify as "general wellness" products — exempt from FDA regulation.
- **Clinical Decision Support (CDS) Guidance (Revised 2026):** Software is exempt from device classification if it meets all four statutory criteria from the 21st Century Cures Act, Section 3060.

### 1.2 The Four CDS Exemption Criteria (Section 520(o)(1)(E))

Software avoids medical device classification when **all four** are met:

1. **Not intended to process medical images or signals** from in vitro diagnostic devices
2. **Intended to display, analyze, or print medical information** about a patient or other medical information
3. **Intended to support or provide recommendations** to healthcare professionals about prevention, diagnosis, or treatment
4. **Enables independent HCP review** — clinicians can independently review the basis for recommendations and are not intended to rely primarily on the software

**Peptide-checker analysis:** Criterion 3 requires the software to be directed at healthcare professionals. Peptide-checker is consumer-facing, which means it does not qualify under CDS exemption — but this is actually *better*, because CDS exemption is for software that *would otherwise be a device*. Peptide-checker is not a device in the first place because it does not diagnose, treat, or make clinical recommendations.

### 1.3 Where Peptide-Checker Falls

Peptide-checker is best classified as a **consumer information platform** — analogous to ConsumerLab.com or Labdoor. It provides:
- Product verification data (what's actually in a product)
- Educational content about peptides
- Third-party testing results and quality ratings

This is **not a medical device** under any current FDA framework, provided the platform:
- Does NOT claim to diagnose any condition
- Does NOT recommend specific treatments or dosages
- Does NOT claim products will cure, treat, or prevent disease
- Does NOT process medical images or diagnostic signals
- Does NOT substitute for professional medical advice

### 1.4 Recent FDA Enforcement Context

The FDA issued 327 warning letters in H2 2025, a 73% increase over H2 2024. Notable digital health enforcement included a July 2025 warning letter to WHOOP for its blood pressure monitoring feature. However, the January 2026 guidance explicitly loosened oversight of wellness-focused tools and information platforms.

**Key principle from 2026 guidance:** "Whether a non-invasive [product] can be considered a general wellness product depends on how its manufacturer or distributor advertises and promotes it, not whether it provides an output that is 'inherently associated' with a disease or condition."

This means **marketing and claims drive regulatory status**, not the underlying data.

### 1.5 21st Century Cures Act Exemptions

The Cures Act (2016) carved out several software categories from FDA device regulation:
- Software for administrative support
- Software for maintaining/encouraging healthy lifestyle (unrelated to disease)
- Electronic patient records
- Software for transferring, storing, converting, or displaying data
- Software that provides clinical decision support meeting the four criteria above

Peptide-checker's information-display function aligns with the "transferring, storing, converting, or displaying data" exemption and potentially the "healthy lifestyle" exemption.

## 2. FTC Regulation of Health Claims

### 2.1 What Constitutes a "Health Claim"

The FTC's December 2022 Health Products Compliance Guidance (the first update in 25 years) applies broadly to **all health-related products and services**, including:
- Dietary supplements
- Health apps and digital tools
- Diagnostic tests
- Health equipment

A "health claim" is any assertion about a product's benefits for health, safety, or bodily function. This includes:
- **Express claims:** "This peptide builds muscle" or "BPC-157 heals injuries"
- **Implied claims:** Showing before/after images, using medical imagery (lab coats, caduceus), or presenting data in a way that implies therapeutic benefit
- **Establishment claims:** "Clinical studies prove..." or "Research shows..."
- **Efficacy claims via testimonials:** User stories implying product effectiveness

### 2.2 Evidence Requirements

The FTC requires "competent and reliable scientific evidence" to support health claims:
- **Gold standard:** Randomized, controlled human clinical trials
- **Minimum requirements:** Control groups, randomization, double blinding, statistically significant results
- **Insufficient alone:** Animal studies, in vitro studies, consumer surveys, mechanism-of-action arguments
- **Totality of evidence:** Must address ALL relevant research, including contradictory studies

### 2.3 Disclaimers: What Works and What Doesn't

**Disclaimers that DO NOT protect:**
- The DSHEA disclaimer ("This statement has not been evaluated by the FDA...") does NOT cure an otherwise deceptive advertisement
- Disclaimers that contradict the express or implied message of the content
- Fine-print disclaimers that are easy to miss
- Hyperlinked disclaimers on social media (considered "avoidable")
- "Results not typical" without disclosing what results consumers can actually expect

**Disclaimers that provide some protection:**
- Clear, conspicuous, and prominent disclaimers that are "difficult to miss"
- Disclaimers integrated into the content (not buried in footnotes)
- Specific, factual qualifications rather than vague hedging

### 2.4 How to Provide Health Information Without Making Claims

The critical distinction for peptide-checker:

**SAFE (information provision):**
- "Published research has examined BPC-157 for [specific endpoints]. Here is what was studied..."
- "This product tested positive for [substance] at [concentration]"
- "The FDA has not approved this peptide for human use"
- Presenting third-party lab results without editorial interpretation about health benefits
- Linking to published research without endorsing conclusions
- Reporting what manufacturers claim vs. what testing found

**UNSAFE (health claims):**
- "BPC-157 heals tendons and reduces inflammation"
- "This peptide is effective for..." or "works for..."
- Ranking products by "effectiveness" for specific health conditions
- Testimonials claiming health improvements from specific products
- Before/after comparisons implying health outcomes
- Using medical imagery or terminology that implies clinical validation

### 2.5 FTC Enforcement History

- **Operation Cure-All:** Multi-agency initiative targeting deceptive online health claims. Challenged products making cure/treatment claims for serious diseases without evidence. Participants included FTC, FDA, state AGs, and Health Canada.
- **Recent trend:** FTC-FDA coordination on supplement and wellness advertising remains active. Enhanced enforcement around influencer marketing and wellness endorsements continues through 2026.

## 3. Supplement and Peptide-Specific Regulation

### 3.1 DSHEA and Peptides

The Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act of 1994 (DSHEA) created the regulatory framework for dietary supplements, but its application to peptides is limited:

- **DSHEA covers:** Vitamins, minerals, herbs, amino acids, and substances used to supplement the diet
- **Peptides under DSHEA:** Only naturally occurring peptides found in food may qualify as dietary ingredients. **Synthetic peptides are NOT classified as dietary ingredients under DSHEA** and cannot be legally sold as supplements
- **Implication for peptide-checker:** The platform is NOT selling peptides, so DSHEA product requirements do not apply directly. However, the platform must be careful not to imply that peptides are legitimate dietary supplements when many are not

### 3.2 Structure/Function Claims vs. Disease Claims

This distinction is critical for any content peptide-checker publishes:

| Structure/Function Claims (Permitted) | Disease Claims (Prohibited) |
|---|---|
| "Calcium builds strong bones" | "Calcium prevents osteoporosis" |
| "Supports immune function" | "Treats immune deficiency" |
| "Promotes joint health" | "Reduces arthritis symptoms" |
| "Supports healthy metabolism" | "Helps treat diabetes" |

**Key rule:** If a statement references identifiable signs or symptoms of a disease, it becomes a disease claim regardless of disclaimers. Context matters — surrounding content can convert a structure/function claim into an implied disease claim.

### 3.3 The Peptide Regulatory Landscape (March 2026)

The peptide market is in significant regulatory flux:

- **February 27, 2026:** HHS Secretary Kennedy announced ~14 of 19 peptides on the FDA Category 2 restricted list will return to Category 1 status, allowing licensed compounding pharmacies to legally prepare them when prescribed
- **Peptides returning to Category 1:** BPC-157, Thymosin Alpha-1, AOD-9604, GHK-Cu, and others
- **March 6, 2026:** Peptide Sciences (largest gray-market research peptide vendor) voluntarily shut down
- **Still restricted:** Semaglutide and tirzepatide compounding remains limited due to FDA-approved alternatives

**Implications for peptide-checker:** This regulatory volatility makes a verification/education platform *more valuable*, not less. Consumers need reliable information precisely because the regulatory landscape is confusing and changing rapidly.

| **ConsumerLab.com** | Subscription-based independent testing | Subscriptions, certification fees ($3K-$7K/product), licensing | LLC structure; publishes results behind paywall; independent third-party evaluator |

## 4. Liability Considerations

### 4.1 Information Platform Liability

Health information platforms face several liability theories:

- **Negligence:** If the platform provides incorrect information and a consumer is harmed. Requires duty of care, breach, causation, and damages.
- **Product liability:** Generally does NOT apply to pure information (First Amendment protections for speech/publishing). However, if information is embedded in a product (e.g., an app that calculates dosages), product liability may attach.
- **Negligent misrepresentation:** If the platform holds itself out as an expert and provides inaccurate information relied upon by consumers.

**Key defense:** Peptide-checker provides *verification data* (lab results, product analysis), not *medical advice*. This is analogous to publishing a newspaper article about food safety — the publisher is generally not liable for consumer decisions based on the reporting.

### 4.2 Section 230 Considerations

If peptide-checker hosts any user-generated content (reviews, comments, forum posts):
- Section 230 immunizes the platform from liability for third-party content
- **BUT:** If the platform uses AI to generate or substantially curate health claims, it may lose Section 230 protection as an "information content provider"
- **BUT:** Section 230 does NOT protect against federal criminal violations or certain IP claims

### 4.3 Professional Liability Insurance

- **Technology E&O (Errors & Omissions):** Covers claims arising from professional services or information provided. Typical cost: $500-$1,000 per employee annually for standard tech E&O.
- **Media liability coverage:** Protects against claims of defamation, invasion of privacy, or intellectual property infringement in published content.
- **Cyber liability:** Covers data breach costs, notification expenses, and regulatory fines.
- **Recommended minimum:** Combined E&O + cyber liability policy with at least $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate.

### 4.4 Terms of Service as Liability Shield

Effective ToS provisions for a health information platform:
- **Disclaimer of medical advice:** "This platform does not provide medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment recommendations"
- **Assumption of risk:** User acknowledges information is for educational purposes only
- **Limitation of liability:** Cap damages at subscription fees paid (or similar nominal amount)
- **Indemnification:** Users agree to hold platform harmless for decisions made based on information
- **Arbitration clause:** Mandatory binding arbitration with class action waiver
- **Choice of law/forum:** Select a jurisdiction favorable to information platforms

**Critical caveat:** Terms of service cannot protect against gross negligence, willful misconduct, or violations of consumer protection statutes. They are one layer in a defense-in-depth strategy.

## 5. Precedent Products: How They Do It

### 5.1 ConsumerLab.com

- **Structure:** ConsumerLab.com, LLC (for-profit limited liability company)
- **What they do:** Purchase supplements at retail, contract third-party ISO-accredited labs for testing, publish results behind a subscription paywall
- **Revenue:** Subscriptions (~$50/year), certification program ($3,000-$7,000/product), licensing, advertising
- **Key regulatory strategies:**
  - Operates as independent evaluator, not a regulatory body
  - Does NOT publicly announce failing products — results are subscriber-only
  - Does NOT accept manufacturer samples for independent reviews
  - Offers voluntary certification programs where manufacturers control whether results are published
  - Has demonstrated willingness to defend itself in court (sued CRN for defamation)
- **Claims approach:** Reports factual test results. Does NOT make therapeutic claims about products.

### 5.2 Labdoor

- **Structure:** For-profit corporation (venture-backed)
- **What they do:** Purchase supplements at retail, test at FDA-registered labs, publish free quality ratings
- **Revenue:** Affiliate marketing (Amazon referral commissions), manufacturer testing/certification fees
- **Key regulatory strategies:**
  - Buys products at retail (same chain of custody as consumers)
  - Uses FDA-registered analytical laboratories
  - Rates on quality metrics (label accuracy, purity, nutritional value) NOT therapeutic efficacy
  - Does NOT make health claims — focuses on "does the label match the contents?"
  - Certification program for manufacturers wanting the Labdoor seal

### 5.3 Clean Label Project

- **Structure:** 501(c)(3) nonprofit
- **What they do:** Test consumer products for environmental/industrial contaminants
- **Revenue:** Certification fees, brand partnerships, awards program
- **Key regulatory strategies:**
  - Tests against California Prop 65 and EU thresholds (stricter than US federal)
  - Uses ISO-accredited labs
  - Maintains consumer chain of custody (buys at retail)
  - Performs unannounced follow-up testing on certified products
  - Does NOT make health claims — focuses on contamination and purity

### 5.4 Common Pattern

All three precedent companies share key regulatory strategies:
1. **Buy retail, don't accept manufacturer samples** for independent reviews
2. **Report factual findings** (lab results, contaminant levels, label accuracy)
3. **Never make therapeutic claims** about products
4. **Use accredited/registered laboratories**
5. **Separate certification programs** (voluntary, manufacturer-funded) from independent reviews (editorially independent)
6. **LLC or corporate structure** for liability protection

## 6. Privacy Requirements

### 6.1 HIPAA: Does Peptide-Checker Need to Comply?

**Almost certainly not.** HIPAA applies to:
- **Covered entities:** Health plans, healthcare clearinghouses, healthcare providers who conduct standard electronic transactions
- **Business associates:** Entities that handle protected health information (PHI) on behalf of covered entities

Peptide-checker is NOT a covered entity and does NOT handle PHI from covered entities. Consumer-facing health information platforms, wellness apps, and supplement testing services are explicitly outside HIPAA scope.

**However:** Being outside HIPAA does not mean no privacy obligations exist.

### 6.2 FTC Health Breach Notification Rule

This rule fills the HIPAA gap for non-covered entities. It applies to "vendors of personal health records" — entities that maintain electronic records of identifiable health information with the capacity to draw from multiple sources, managed by the individual.

**Peptide-checker analysis:** If the platform is purely informational (provides information about peptide products without maintaining individual health records or tracking personal health data), the Health Breach Notification Rule likely does NOT apply. The FTC has clarified that "a health advice application or website that is purely informational and does not maintain a mechanism for consumers to track or record their information" is excluded.

**Risk factor:** If peptide-checker adds features like:
- User profiles tracking what peptides they use
- Personal health journals or logs
- Integration with health data from other sources
- Personalized recommendations based on health conditions

...then the Health Breach Notification Rule would likely apply, requiring breach notification to individuals, the FTC, and potentially media.

### 6.3 Washington My Health My Data Act (MHMDA)

This is the **highest-risk privacy law** for peptide-checker. Key facts:

- **No revenue or user thresholds:** Applies to ANY entity conducting business in Washington or targeting Washington consumers, regardless of size
- **Extremely broad definition:** "Consumer health data" includes virtually any data about health, wellness, nutrition, fitness, or related topics — or data used to *infer* such information
- **Extraterritorial reach:** Applies to entities outside Washington if they collect data from Washington residents
- **Private right of action:** Individual consumers can sue; violations are per se unfair trade practices under Washington CPA
- **Treble damages:** Courts may award up to $25,000 in treble damages

**Requirements if triggered:**
- Maintain a **separate** consumer health data privacy policy
- Obtain **opt-in consent** for data collection/sharing not necessary to provide the requested service
- Implement **reasonable security measures**
- Respond to data subject access and deletion requests
- Execute appropriate data processing agreements with third parties

**Peptide-checker trigger analysis:** If the platform collects any data about what peptides users are researching, purchasing, or using — even search queries or browsing behavior on the site — this likely constitutes "consumer health data" under MHMDA.

### 6.4 CCPA/CPRA (California)

Applies to for-profit businesses meeting ANY of:
- Gross annual revenue > $26.625 million
- Buy/sell/share personal information of 100,000+ consumers or households
- Derive 50%+ of revenue from selling/sharing personal information

**Peptide-checker at launch:** Likely below thresholds. But plan for compliance as the platform grows.

As of January 1, 2026, new CCPA regulations require risk assessments for processing sensitive personal information and automated decision-making transparency.

### 6.5 Other State Laws

A patchwork of state consumer health data laws is emerging. Connecticut, Nevada, and other states have enacted or proposed similar health data protection laws. Design privacy practices to the highest standard (MHMDA) to ensure broad compliance.

## 7. Practical Compliance Checklist for Peptide-Checker

### Day 1 Requirements (Before Launch)

#### Legal Entity
- [ ] Form an **LLC** (preferably in Delaware or Wyoming for strongest liability protections)
- [ ] Obtain **EIN** and open separate business bank account
- [ ] **Never commingle personal and business funds** (preserves LLC liability shield)
- [ ] Consider whether a single-member LLC is sufficient or whether a multi-member LLC or series LLC provides better protection

#### Insurance
- [ ] Obtain **Technology E&O insurance** ($1M/$2M minimum)
- [ ] Add **cyber liability coverage** (covers data breach notification costs)
- [ ] Consider **media liability rider** (covers defamation/IP claims in published content)
- [ ] Budget: $2,000-$5,000/year for a combined policy at startup stage

#### Terms of Service
- [ ] Draft comprehensive ToS including:
  - Medical advice disclaimer ("This platform does not provide medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment")
  - "For educational and informational purposes only" statement
  - Limitation of liability (cap at fees paid)
  - Indemnification clause
  - Binding arbitration with class action waiver
  - Choice of law and forum selection
  - User acknowledgment that peptide regulatory status is evolving
  - Disclaimer that platform does not endorse, recommend, or encourage use of any specific product
- [ ] Require **affirmative acceptance** (checkbox, not browse-wrap)

#### Privacy Policy
- [ ] Draft a **general privacy policy** covering all data collection
- [ ] Draft a **separate consumer health data privacy policy** (MHMDA compliance)
- [ ] Both policies must disclose:
  - What data is collected
  - How it is used
  - Who it is shared with
  - How long it is retained
  - How users can access/delete their data
- [ ] Implement **opt-in consent** for any data collection beyond what's necessary to provide the service
- [ ] Do NOT use third-party analytics (Google Analytics, Meta Pixel) on health-related pages without explicit consent

#### Content Guidelines
- [ ] Establish **editorial guidelines** prohibiting:
  - Disease claims ("treats," "cures," "prevents," "diagnoses")
  - Efficacy claims ("effective for," "works for," "proven to")
  - Dosage recommendations
  - Treatment protocols
  - Before/after comparisons implying health outcomes
- [ ] **Permitted content patterns:**
  - "Published research has examined [peptide] for [endpoint]"
  - "This product was tested and found to contain [substance] at [concentration]"
  - "The manufacturer claims [X]. Our testing found [Y]"
  - "The FDA has/has not approved this for human use"
  - "Consult a healthcare professional before using any peptide product"
- [ ] Add prominent disclaimer on every page: "This platform provides product verification data and educational information. It does not provide medical advice. Consult a qualified healthcare professional."
- [ ] Include on every product review page: "[Product] has not been evaluated by the FDA for safety or efficacy. This review reports laboratory testing results only."

### Pre-Revenue / Growth Phase

#### Data Minimization
- [ ] Collect **minimum necessary data** — do not collect health conditions, medical history, or treatment plans
- [ ] If users create accounts, do NOT require health-related information
- [ ] Implement **data retention limits** — delete what you don't need
- [ ] If you track search queries, **anonymize** them and do not link to user accounts

#### Security Measures
- [ ] Implement **encryption in transit** (TLS/HTTPS) and at rest
- [ ] Use **access controls** limiting who can view user data
- [ ] Maintain **security incident response plan** (required for MHMDA and FTC HBNR if triggered)
- [ ] Regular security assessments

#### Content Review Process
- [ ] Every piece of content reviewed for **prohibited claims** before publication
- [ ] Document the review process (demonstrates good faith if challenged)
- [ ] If using AI to generate content, **human review required** before publication
- [ ] Maintain **editorial independence** from any manufacturer relationships

### Scale Phase

- [ ] Reassess CCPA applicability as revenue and user counts grow
- [ ] Consider CCPA **risk assessments** for any automated decision-making
- [ ] If adding user-generated content (reviews, forums), implement **content moderation** for health claims
- [ ] If entering affiliate/certification relationships with manufacturers, maintain **clear separation** between independent reviews and paid programs
- [ ] Annual legal review of regulatory landscape (peptide regulation is changing rapidly)
- [ ] Monitor for new state health data privacy laws

## 9. The Critical Distinction: Information vs. Advice

The entire regulatory strategy for peptide-checker hinges on one principle:

**INFORMATION platforms report facts. ADVICE platforms make recommendations.**

| Information (Safe) | Advice (Risky) |
|---|---|
| "Lab tests found this product contains 98% of labeled BPC-157" | "This product is good for injury recovery" |
| "Three published studies examined this peptide for X endpoint" | "Research proves this peptide works for X" |
| "This product failed purity testing for heavy metals" | "Don't use this product because it's dangerous" |
| "The FDA categorizes this peptide as Category 2" | "This peptide is safe because the FDA is reconsidering it" |
| "Manufacturer claims X. Our testing found Y." | "This manufacturer is lying about their product" |

The left column is what ConsumerLab, Labdoor, and Clean Label Project do. The right column is what gets companies FTC warning letters.
