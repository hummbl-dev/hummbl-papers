## Executive Summary

The peptide regulatory landscape in early 2026 is defined by a dramatic pendulum swing. After the FDA placed 19 peptides on the Category 2 restricted list in September 2023 -- effectively banning their compounding -- HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. announced on February 27, 2026, that approximately 14 of those 19 peptides would be reclassified back to Category 1, restoring access through licensed compounding pharmacies with a physician's prescription. However, **as of March 2026, no formal FDA rulemaking has been published**, and the legal status remains technically unchanged. Simultaneously, the gray-market "research use only" peptide industry is collapsing: Peptide Sciences, the largest vendor, voluntarily shut down on March 6, 2026, following DOJ prosecutions, FDA warning letters, and pharmaceutical company litigation. WADA continues to prohibit most performance-related peptides, and international regulators (Australia's TGA, UK's MHRA, Health Canada, EMA) maintain tight controls on therapeutic peptide access. For a peptide verification business, the regulatory turbulence creates both urgency and opportunity: consumers need trustworthy information more than ever, but the legal ground is shifting under every category.

### Five Peptides Removed from Category 2 (September 2024)

The nominators who originally proposed these for Category 2 withdrew their submissions, and the PCAC subsequently voted against their inclusion:

1. AOD-9604
2. CJC-1295
3. Ipamorelin Acetate
4. Thymosin Alpha-1
5. Selank Acetate

These are now listed under "Other Bulk Drug Substances That May Present Significant Safety Risks" -- a less restrictive category, though they remain in regulatory limbo pending further action.

### Peptides Expected to Remain Category 2

Based on the gap between "14 of 19" announced for reclassification, approximately 5 peptides may remain restricted. Industry analysis suggests these are likely:

- **Ibutamoren Mesylate (MK-677)** -- oral GH secretagogue with documented side effects
- **Melanotan II** -- significant cardiovascular and melanoma risk concerns
- **PEG-MGF** -- limited safety data, performance enhancement associations
- **Dihexa Acetate** -- potent nootropic with minimal human safety data
- **Cathelicidin LL-37** -- antimicrobial peptide with immunogenic concerns

**Critical caveat:** The official FDA list of which 14 will be reclassified has not been published. The above is based on industry-informed predictions only.

## FDA Enforcement: Key Actions

### DOJ Prosecution: Tailor Made Compounding
The Department of Justice prosecuted Tailor Made Compounding LLC for distributing unapproved peptides including BPC-157, resulting in a **$1.79 million forfeiture**.

### Warning Letter Campaign
In 2025, the FDA issued **50+ warning letters in a single month** to peptide vendors, compounding pharmacies, and telehealth platforms. Specific targets included:
- Companies making therapeutic health claims about research peptides
- Vendors implying products were interchangeable with FDA-approved drugs
- Social media influencer marketing partnerships implying therapeutic benefits

### GLP-1 Compounding Crackdown
Despite the official end of semaglutide and tirzepatide shortages, prescriptions for compounded versions **increased** after the shortage ended (per IQVIA data, Oct 2025). Approximately 80% of compounded semaglutide/tirzepatide prescriptions now include supplemental ingredients (e.g., B vitamins) to argue they are not exact copies -- a legal strategy of uncertain validity.

- **7+ research peptide companies** shut down in 2025 alone
- **Peptide Sciences** voluntarily ceased operations on March 6, 2026
- Contributing factors: FDA enforcement, DOJ criminal prosecutions, Big Pharma patent litigation (Eli Lilly, Novo Nordisk), quality failures (Finnrick testing found E-rated retatrutide across 37 samples)

## HHS Category 2 Reclassification: Detailed Analysis

### What Is Category 2?

Under the FDA's interim policy for bulk drug substances used in compounding:

- **Category 1:** Substances that can be used by compounding pharmacies (with a valid prescription)
- **Category 2:** Substances that "raise significant safety concerns" and are restricted from compounding use pending further evaluation by the Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee (PCAC)
- **Category 3:** Substances that may be used under certain conditions

Category 2 designation is an **interim administrative categorization**, not a formal rulemaking under the Administrative Procedure Act. This distinction matters legally: Kennedy's assertion that the FDA "illegally" reclassified the peptides reflects a debatable interpretation of agency authority.

### Current Status (March 2026)

Despite the February 27, 2026 announcement:

- **No FDA rule has changed**
- **No Federal Register notice has been issued**
- **No statute has been amended**
- **No official list of the 14 peptides has been published**

The announcement was made during a podcast appearance (Joe Rogan Experience #2461), not through official regulatory channels. Until the FDA formally updates the Category 2 list, the legal status of these peptides for compounding remains technically unchanged.

### What Would Reclassification Mean?

If formalized, Category 1 status would:
- Allow licensed 503A and 503B compounding pharmacies to prepare these peptides
- Require a valid physician prescription for each patient
- **Not** make peptides available over the counter
- **Not** constitute FDA approval of the peptides as drugs
- **Not** legalize the gray-market "research use only" sales model

## WADA 2026 Prohibited List: Peptide Status

The 2026 WADA Prohibited List took effect January 1, 2026. Changes from 2025 were primarily clarifications rather than new additions.

### Prohibited Peptides by Category

#### S0 -- Non-Approved Substances (Banned at All Times)
Any pharmacological substance not addressed by subsequent sections and with no current approval by any governmental regulatory health authority for human therapeutic use is prohibited. This captures:
- **BPC-157** (added 2022)
- **TB-500 / Thymosin Beta-4**
- **MOTS-C**
- **Epitalon**
- **Semax / Selank**
- **KPV**
- Most other non-approved research peptides

#### S2 -- Peptide Hormones, Growth Factors, Related Substances and Mimetics (Banned at All Times)

**Growth Hormone (GH) and fragments:**
- GH analogues: lonapegsomatropin, somapacitan, somatrogon
- GH fragments: **AOD-9604**, hGH 176-191

**Growth Hormone Releasing Hormones (GHRH) and analogues:**
- **CJC-1293, CJC-1295**, sermorelin, tesamorelin

**Growth Hormone Secretagogues (GHS):**
- Anamorelin, capromorelin
- **Ibutamoren (MK-677)**
- **Ipamorelin**
- Lenomorelin (ghrelin), macimorelin, tabimorelin

**Other prohibited peptide hormones:**
- Erythropoietin (EPO) and analogues
- **IGF-1** and analogues
- **Kisspeptin** and analogues
- **Thymosin Beta-4** and derivatives

#### Key 2025-to-2026 Changes
- No new peptide categories were added
- Additional examples and clarifications were provided within existing categories
- Carbon monoxide prohibition (M1.4) was a notable non-peptide addition

### Country-by-Country Details

#### Australia
- **Scheduling:** BPC-157, TB-500, and growth hormone secretagogues are Schedule 4 (prescription only). Growth hormone (somatropin) is Schedule 8 (controlled drug). Certain SARMs are Schedule 9 (prohibited).
- **Import rules:** Personal Importation Scheme permits max 3-month supply with valid prescription from Australian-registered doctor. Schedule 8/9 substances completely excluded.
- **Enforcement:** Australian Border Force works with TGA to intercept peptide shipments; seizures have increased significantly since 2022. AHPRA investigating clinics prescribing outside accepted indications.
- **Penalties:** Unauthorized importation fines exceeding AUD $200,000 under the Customs Act 1901. In NSW, Schedule 4 possession without prescription: fines up to AUD $11,000 and/or imprisonment up to 2 years.
- **2025-2026 changes:** Targeted compounding pharmacy audits; increased border seizures; online seller crackdowns.

#### United Kingdom
- **Legal framework:** Most research peptides (BPC-157, GHK-Cu, TB-500) remain unscheduled under the Misuse of Drugs Act 1971 as of January 1, 2026.
- **Sale conditions:** Legal to buy and sell when supplied for research and development purposes. Must be labeled "for research purposes only" or "not for human consumption."
- **MHRA enforcement:** Actively enforces against companies selling unlicensed medicines. Has shut down operations selling peptides as health supplements or weight loss products. Generally does not pursue suppliers selling clearly labeled research chemicals.
- **Future outlook:** Anticipated 2026 regulatory changes could either broaden access for legitimate use or tighten restrictions.

#### Canada
- **Framework:** Peptides intended for human therapeutic use require Health Canada approval. Compounding is permitted only when a patient's medical need cannot be met by an approved drug, or during a declared shortage.
- **GLP-1 enforcement:** Health Canada considers pharmacy-compounded GLP-1s to be unauthorized manufacturing. January 2025: requested recall of compounded semaglutide/pyridoxine products due to use of synthetic (non-approved) semaglutide API.
- **Research peptides:** Exist in a regulatory gray area similar to the US. Health Canada has issued warnings but systematic enforcement against research peptide vendors is less aggressive than FDA.

#### European Union
- **EMA guidelines:** Published updated guidelines on development and manufacture of synthetic peptides in December 2025 (effective June 1, 2026).
- **Approval pathway:** Synthetically produced follow-on peptide products are not eligible for the biosimilar pathway; require traditional generic or hybrid pathway approval.
- **Member state variation:** Individual EU member states may impose additional restrictions. Research chemical sales generally permitted with appropriate labeling.
- **Enforcement:** Focused on pharmaceutical manufacturing standards rather than consumer research chemical markets, though individual member states vary in enforcement intensity.

## Consumer Legal Status Guide (United States, March 2026)

### What Can a US Consumer Legally Buy?

#### Legally Available with Prescription
- **FDA-approved peptide drugs:** Semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy), tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound), tesamorelin (Egrifta), and a small number of others
- **Category 1 compounded peptides:** Available from licensed 503A/503B pharmacies with a valid physician prescription (note: the 5 peptides withdrawn from Cat 2 in Sep 2024 are in a transitional status)
- **Cosmetic peptides:** Topical GHK-Cu, Matrixyl, Argireline (available OTC in skincare products)

#### Gray Area (Legal Risk)
- **"Research use only" peptides:** Purchasing for legitimate laboratory research is technically legal. However:
  - Self-administration is not sanctioned by the FDA
  - The gray-market supply chain is collapsing (Peptide Sciences shutdown, 7+ vendors closed in 2025)
  - FDA warning letters target vendors implying therapeutic use
  - Independent testing finds ~8% endotoxin contamination rate; some samples contain zero labeled compound
  - State AGs increasingly pursue consumer protection actions

#### Requires Prescription (Not Legally Available OTC)
- All 19 Category 2 peptides when intended for human use
- Any compounded peptide preparation
- GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide, tirzepatide) -- compounding no longer permitted post-shortage

#### Definitively Illegal
- **Selling peptides with therapeutic claims** without FDA approval
- **Compounding Category 2 peptides** for human use (as of March 2026)
- **Compounding semaglutide/tirzepatide** (shortage resolved; grace periods expired)
- **Importing counterfeit or adulterated peptides** for sale
- **Marketing peptides as dietary supplements** (peptides injected subcutaneously do not qualify as dietary supplements under DSHEA)

### What Is Likely Changing

If the Kennedy reclassification is formalized:
- ~14 peptides return to Category 1 status
- Licensed compounding pharmacies can prepare them with valid prescriptions
- **This does NOT legalize over-the-counter sales or gray-market "research" peptides**
- Consumers will still need a physician relationship and prescription
- Quality oversight through compounding pharmacy standards (USP <797>, USP <800>)

## Implications for Peptide Verification Business

### The Core Opportunity

The current regulatory environment creates a **verification gap** that is wider than at any point in the peptide market's history:

1. **Gray market collapsing, legitimate market not yet open.** The period between gray-market shutdown and formal Category 1 reclassification is a dangerous interstitial period where consumers may turn to unknown, unverified sources.

2. **Quality concerns are documented and severe.** Finnrick testing data shows meaningful contamination and mislabeling rates. Independent testing found ~8% endotoxin contamination across vendors. The Tailor Made Compounding prosecution ($1.79M forfeiture) demonstrates the stakes.

3. **Consumers are confused.** The Kennedy announcement created demand before any actual quality oversight is in place. Online sellers are already using the announcement to market products that remain legally unregulated.

### Strategic Considerations

| Factor | Impact on Peptide Checker |
|--------|--------------------------|
| **Category 1 reclassification** | Increases legitimate compounding market; verification of compounding pharmacy quality becomes valuable |
| **Gray market collapse** | Reduces the "research peptide" market but increases desperation buying from unknown sources |
| **State-level enforcement variation** | Creates need for state-specific regulatory guidance within the tool |
| **WADA prohibition** | Athletes remain a distinct user segment needing doping risk information |
| **International variation** | Opportunity for international expansion; regulatory database is a differentiator |
| **Quality testing data** | Third-party testing results (Finnrick, Janoshik) are high-value data for verification |

### Regulatory Risk to Business

- **If peptides move to Category 1:** Legitimate compounding pharmacies become the primary supply chain. Verification shifts from "is this real?" to "is this pharmacy compliant?"
- **If reclassification stalls:** Extended gray-market period with higher consumer risk. Verification of research peptide sources remains the core use case.
- **If enforcement intensifies:** Potential liability concerns for any tool that could be construed as facilitating access to unapproved drugs. Careful positioning as an information/safety tool is essential.

### Recommended Positioning

A peptide verification tool should:
1. **Not recommend or direct users to purchase** any specific product
2. **Provide regulatory status information** clearly and accurately
3. **Surface quality testing data** (Finnrick, Janoshik, published studies) as a public health service
4. **Include WADA status** for athlete-relevant peptides
5. **Track state-level enforcement** to provide jurisdiction-specific guidance
6. **Update in real-time** as the Category 2 reclassification proceeds (or stalls)
