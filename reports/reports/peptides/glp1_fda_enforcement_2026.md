# RQ-PEP-002: FDA Enforcement on Compounded Semaglutide, Tirzepatide, and GLP-1 Agonists

**Research ID:** RQ-PEP-002
**Date:** 2026-03-23
**Domain:** Peptide Regulation
**Status:** Completed

---

## Executive Summary

The GLP-1 compounding market has entered a decisive enforcement phase. After the FDA resolved the semaglutide shortage on February 21, 2025 (tirzepatide in October/December 2024), the legal basis for most compounding evaporated. The FDA issued 55+ warning letters in September 2025, Novo Nordisk and Eli Lilly launched aggressive litigation campaigns, and 38 state attorneys general called for federal action. A landmark JMIR study (Ashraf et al., 2024) found online semaglutide products contained only 7.7-14.4% purity versus 99% claimed, with endotoxin contamination in all samples. As of early 2026, the FDA's FAERS database shows 900+ adverse event reports including 17 deaths linked to compounded GLP-1 products, with dosing errors causing 5-20x overdoses being a primary driver of hospitalizations. This crisis directly validates the need for a peptide verification service.

## 1. FDA Enforcement Actions (2024-2026)

### 1.1 Shortage Designations and Resolution Timeline

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2022 | Semaglutide shortage begins due to Ozempic/Wegovy demand surge |
| Oct 2, 2024 | FDA removes tirzepatide from shortage list (later challenged in court) |
| Dec 2024 | FDA re-affirms tirzepatide shortage resolved after court remand |
| Feb 21, 2025 | FDA removes semaglutide from shortage list, ending ~3-year shortage |
| Apr 22, 2025 | Enforcement discretion ends for 503A pharmacies (semaglutide) |
| May 22, 2025 | Enforcement discretion ends for 503B outsourcing facilities (semaglutide) |

**Source:** [FDA clarifies policies for compounders as national GLP-1 supply begins to stabilize](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-safety-and-availability/fda-clarifies-policies-compounders-national-glp-1-supply-begins-stabilize)

### 1.2 The 503A vs 503B Legal Framework

- **503A (State-Licensed Pharmacies):** May compound patient-specific prescriptions based on individual prescriber orders. Cannot compound "essentially a copy" of a commercially available drug unless that drug is on the FDA shortage list. Enforcement discretion for semaglutide ended April 22, 2025.
- **503B (Outsourcing Facilities):** May compound without patient-specific prescriptions but must register with FDA and comply with cGMP. Enforcement discretion ended May 22, 2025.
- **Key constraint:** Neither category may compound an "essential copy" of a commercially available drug once the shortage is resolved, unless the compounder can demonstrate a clinical difference (e.g., allergy to an inactive ingredient in the branded product).

**Source:** [The FDA Removes Semaglutide from the Drug Shortage List (Burr & Forman)](https://www.burr.com/newsroom/articles/the-fda-removes-semaglutide-from-the-drug-shortage-list); [Sheppard Health Law](https://www.sheppardhealthlaw.com/2025/03/articles/fda/fdas-semaglutide-shortage-resolution-legal-implications-and-risks-for-compounding-pharmacies/)

### 1.3 Warning Letters (September 2025)

On September 9-16, 2025, the FDA issued **55+ warning letters** to U.S. and international companies compounding or manufacturing GLP-1 products. Key violations cited:

- False claims that compounded products are "generic versions" of Ozempic/Wegovy
- Claims of containing the "same active ingredient" as FDA-approved drugs
- Marketing compounded products without adequate safety disclosures
- 22% of all CDER warning letters in FY2025 targeted telehealth/compounding GLP-1 claims

**Source:** [Wilson Sonsini analysis](https://www.wsgr.com/en/insights/fda-sends-warning-letters-to-more-than-50-glp-1-compounders-and-manufacturers.html); [Holland & Knight](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2025/09/fda-hhs-taking-action-against-telehealths-compounded-drug-advertising)

### 1.4 Recalls and Seizures

- **August 2024:** 15,000+ vials of ProRx compounded semaglutide and tirzepatide recalled due to lack of sterility assurance.
- **January 2025:** Exton, Pennsylvania compounding pharmacy/outsourcing facility received warning letter for cGMP violations producing adulterated drugs.
- **Ongoing:** FDA aware of fraudulent compounded products with fake pharmacy names on labels, and products from non-existent compounding facilities.

**Source:** [MedShadow Foundation](https://medshadow.org/drug-updates-recalls/fda-drug-recalls-and-warnings-recalls-for-compounded-semaglutide-and-tirzepatide-wegovy-facility-warning-letters/); [FDA Compounding Risk Alerts](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/human-drug-compounding/compounding-risk-alerts)

### 1.5 February 2026 Enforcement Posture

The FDA signaled intent to take action against non-FDA-approved GLP-1 drugs with clear enforcement priorities:
1. Compounding without documented medical necessity
2. Unlicensed manufacturing operations
3. Misleading marketing and advertising
4. Improper storage and shipment practices

Despite prescriptions for compounded semaglutide **increasing** after the shortage ended (per IQVIA October 2025 report), the FDA has not imposed a blanket prohibition on lawful patient-specific compounding.

**Source:** [Spencer Fane](https://www.spencerfane.com/insight/fda-and-novo-nordisk-warn-of-glp-1-telehealth-compounding-take-down-whats-next/); [BIPC](https://www.bipc.com/oral-semaglutide-and-the-glp-1-compounding-reckoning-from-regulatory-ambiguity-to-fda-enforcement,-doj-referrals,-and-novo-nordisk%E2%80%99s-case-against-hims-and-hers)

## 2. JMIR Purity Study: Detailed Analysis

### 2.1 Full Citation

> Ashraf AR, Mackey TK, Vida RG, Kulcsar G, Schmidt J, Balazs O, Domian BM, Li J, Csako I, Fittler A. **"Multifactor Quality and Safety Analysis of Semaglutide Products Sold by Online Sellers Without a Prescription: Market Surveillance, Content Analysis, and Product Purchase Evaluation Study."** *Journal of Medical Internet Research.* 2024;26:e65440. Published November 7, 2024. DOI: [10.2196/65440](https://doi.org/10.2196/65440)

**PubMed ID:** 39509151 | **PMC:** PMC11582493

### 2.2 Methodology

- **Analytical instrument:** Thermo Ultimate 3000 UHPLC system with Luna Omega PS-C18 reversed-phase column coupled to a Bruker Maxis 4G UltraHigh Resolution Quadrupole Time of Flight (Q-TOF) mass spectrometer (LC-MS)
- **Sterility testing:** Per European and US Pharmacopoeia standards
- **Endotoxin measurement:** Kinetic turbidimetry
- **Visual inspection:** Adapted FIP (International Pharmaceutical Federation) checklist
- **Market surveillance:** Search engine results page analysis, website content assessment, domain traffic analytics

### 2.3 Samples

- **6 vendors targeted** for test purchases (selected as highest-link-count vendors offering semaglutide without prescription at lowest prices)
- **3 injection vials delivered** (from BiotechPeptides, SemaSpace, USChemLabs)
- **3 Ozempic prefilled pens never received** (non-delivery e-commerce scams from GeniusPharmacy, PureMedsOnline, WeightCrunchShop)

### 2.4 Key Findings

| Vendor | Claimed Purity | Measured Purity | Endotoxin (EU/mg) | Content vs Label |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|-------------------|------------------|
| SemaSpace | 99% | **14.37%** | **8.95** | +28.56% |
| USChemLabs | 99% | **8.97%** | <2.87 | +33.6% (est.) |
| BiotechPeptides | 99% | **7.70%** | <2.16 | +38.69% |

Critical findings:
- **Purity 7.7-14.4%** versus 99% claimed (85-92% deviation from label)
- **Endotoxin detected in all samples** (2.16-8.95 EU/mg)
- **Content exceeded labeled amounts by 28.6-38.7%** (overdose risk)
- **59-63% non-compliance** on visual inspection criteria
- No peptide-like impurities detected; samples were sterile at time of testing
- All vials classified as "probable substandard and falsified products"

### 2.5 Follow-Up Research

- **Expert Opinion on Drug Safety (2025):** Pharmacovigilance study using FAERS data (Tandfonline DOI: 10.1080/14740338.2025.2499670) analyzed 81,078 GLP-1 RA reports; 707 involved compounded products. Found higher reporting odds ratios for abdominal pain, diarrhea, nausea, suicidality, and cholecystitis in compounded formulations.
- **Journal of the Endocrine Society (2025):** "Global Rise of Compounded Weight-Loss Medicines: A Worrisome Trend" - editorial/review highlighting the international scope of the problem.
- No direct replication of the Ashraf et al. purity testing has been published as of March 2026, representing a significant gap.

**Source:** [JMIR full text](https://www.jmir.org/2024/1/e65440); [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11582493/); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40285721/)

## 3. Consumer Risk Assessment

### 3.1 Documented Adverse Events

**FAERS Data (as of Dec 31, 2024):**
- **900+ adverse event reports** associated with compounded semaglutide and tirzepatide
- **17 deaths** reported
- **~10 deaths and ~100 hospitalizations** estimated by FDA to be linked specifically to dosing errors
- **605 adverse event reports** for compounded semaglutide alone (as of July 31, 2025)

**Primary adverse events reported:**
- Gastrointestinal: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, constipation
- Acute pancreatitis
- Dehydration (from severe GI symptoms)
- Gallstones / cholecystitis
- Suicidality (elevated reporting odds in compounded vs non-compounded)

**Source:** [FDA Concerns with Unapproved GLP-1 Drugs](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/postmarket-drug-safety-information-patients-and-providers/fdas-concerns-unapproved-glp-1-drugs-used-weight-loss); [Endocrinology Advisor](https://www.endocrinologyadvisor.com/cch/compounded-glp-1-receptor-agonists-tirzepatide-faers/)

### 3.2 Contamination and Quality Failures

| Risk Category | Details |
|--------------|---------|
| **Endotoxin contamination** | Detected in all JMIR samples (2.16-8.95 EU/mg); can cause fever, sepsis-like symptoms |
| **Wrong salt form** | Semaglutide sodium and semaglutide acetate used instead of semaglutide base; unknown pharmacokinetic equivalence |
| **Dosing inconsistency** | Content 28-39% above labeled amounts; multi-dose vials cause confusion between mg, mL, and "units" |
| **Aggregation/impurities** | Protein aggregates and residual host cell proteins can provoke immune response |
| **Degradation** | Improper shipping/storage (temperature excursions) degrades peptide integrity |
| **Counterfeits** | Fake pharmacy names on labels; products from non-existent facilities |
| **Sterility failures** | ProRx 15,000-vial recall (Aug 2024) due to sterility assurance failure |

**Compounded products showed dramatically elevated reporting odds ratios (FAERS study):**
- Preparation errors: **48.92x** higher
- Contamination: **19.00x** higher
- Compounding/manufacturing issues: **8.51x** higher

### 3.3 Price Comparison

| Product | Monthly Cost | FDA Approved? |
|---------|-------------|---------------|
| Ozempic (semaglutide) | $1,300-$1,900 | Yes |
| Wegovy (semaglutide) | $1,300-$1,900 | Yes |
| Mounjaro (tirzepatide) | ~$1,493 | Yes |
| Zepbound (tirzepatide) | ~$1,519 | Yes |
| Compounded semaglutide (telehealth) | $150-$400 | **No** |
| Compounded tirzepatide (telehealth) | $200-$500 | **No** |
| Eli Lilly savings card (Mounjaro) | As low as $25/month | Yes (with program) |

The 75-90% cost difference is the primary driver of consumer demand for compounded products.

**Source:** [Peterson-KFF Health System Tracker](https://www.healthsystemtracker.org/brief/prices-of-drugs-for-weight-loss-in-the-us-and-peer-nations/); [SingleCare](https://www.singlecare.com/blog/tirzepatide-vs-semaglutide-cost/)

### 3.4 Consumer Access Channels

**Telehealth platforms (major):**
- **Hims & Hers** - Compounded semaglutide from $199/month (6-month plan); sued by Novo Nordisk Feb 2026, settled Mar 2026 via collaboration deal
- **Ro** - Partners with Eli Lilly since 2024; offers both branded and compounded
- **Noom, Calibrate, Sesame, WeightWatchers** - Various GLP-1 access programs
- **Teladoc** - Weight loss program including GLP-1 access

**Gray market / illicit channels:**
- Direct-to-consumer websites selling without prescription (SemaSpace, USChemLabs, BiotechPeptides per JMIR study)
- Research chemical vendors relabeling peptides
- International sources (China, Turkey, India per AG coalition letter)
- Social media marketplaces and influencer promotion

**Source:** [STAT News on Hims & Hers](https://www.statnews.com/2026/02/23/him-hers-earnings-2026-outlook-compounded-semaglutide/); [Healthcare Brew](https://www.healthcare-brew.com/stories/2026/03/23/fda-crackdown-unapproved-glp-1s)

## 4. Novo Nordisk and Eli Lilly Legal Actions

### 4.1 Novo Nordisk Litigation Campaign

| Date | Action | Target |
|------|--------|--------|
| Mar 5, 2025 | Granted intervention in OFA v. FDA | Outsourcing Facilities Association |
| Aug 4, 2025 | Filed lawsuits against 12 defendants | Multiple pharmacies including Axtell's Rite-Value, Link Pharmacy |
| Feb 8, 2026 | Cease-and-desist letter | Hims & Hers |
| Feb 9, 2026 | Patent lawsuit (U.S. Patent No. 8,129,343) | Hims & Hers Health |
| Mar 9, 2026 | **Voluntarily dismissed lawsuit** | Hims & Hers (collaboration deal announced) |

**Patent strategy:** U.S. Patent 8,129,343 covers the semaglutide compound, pharmaceutical compositions containing it, and methods of treatment. Composition of matter patents expire December 2031 in the U.S.

**Novo Nordisk 2024 revenue context:** $8B Wegovy + $16.9B Ozempic = $24.9B in semaglutide sales.

### 4.2 Eli Lilly Litigation Campaign

| Date | Action | Target |
|------|--------|--------|
| ~Jan 2025 | Granted intervention in OFA v. FDA | Outsourcing Facilities Association |
| Mar 5, 2025 | OFA injunction denied by Judge Pittman | N/A (vindication of FDA's shortage resolution) |
| Apr 1, 2025 | First lawsuits filed | Strive Pharmacy, Empower Pharmacy, others |
| Mid-2025 | "Barrage of lawsuits" against telehealth providers | Multiple "bad actors" |
| Ongoing | Cease-and-desist letters to entire supply chain | Pharmacies, clinics, prescribers |

**Legal theories employed:** Trademark infringement, consumer protection violations, deceptive trade practices (not patent-based for most suits).

### 4.3 Outsourcing Facilities Association v. FDA

- **Court:** U.S. District Court, Northern District of Texas (Judge Mark T. Pittman)
- **OFA claim:** FDA's removal of tirzepatide and semaglutide from shortage lists was "reckless and arbitrary"
- **Tirzepatide ruling:** Preliminary injunction denied March 5, 2025
- **Semaglutide challenge:** Filed February 24, 2025 (separate suit challenging semaglutide shortage resolution)
- **Outcome:** FDA's shortage determinations upheld; compounders lost legal basis for continued bulk production

**Source:** [BioSpace](https://www.biospace.com/business/novo-enters-the-ring-in-legal-fight-against-glp-1-compounders); [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/02/09/novo-nordisk-sues-hims-hers-compounded-obesity-drugs.html); [CNBC settlement](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/09/novo-nordisk-ends-legal-proceedings-hims-hers-compounded-weight-loss-drugs.html); [ABA](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/health_law/news/2025/1/fda-removes-tirzepatide-injection-from-shortage-list/)

### 4.4 The Hims & Hers Resolution (March 2026)

The Novo Nordisk v. Hims & Hers case is particularly significant. After Hims launched a $49 compounded semaglutide pill (withdrawn under legal pressure), Novo sued on February 9, 2026. Within one month, Novo voluntarily dismissed the case after announcing a **collaboration** where Hims & Hers agreed to sell FDA-approved Novo Nordisk GLP-1 medications on its platform. This signals a potential industry pattern: litigation as leverage for distribution partnerships.

**Source:** [PBS News](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/nation/novo-nordisk-drops-lawsuit-against-hims-hers-over-off-brand-weight-loss-medications-enter-collaboration)

## 5. State-Level Enforcement

### 5.1 38-State Coalition (February 2025)

In February 2025, **38 state and territory attorneys general** signed a joint letter to the FDA demanding federal action against counterfeit GLP-1 drugs entering the U.S. supply chain from China, Turkey, India, and other countries. This unprecedented coalition signaled that states viewed the FDA's pace of enforcement as insufficient.

**Source:** [NAAG](https://www.naag.org/press-releases/state-and-territory-attorneys-general-urge-fda-to-take-action-against-counterfeit-and-illegally-sold-glp-1-drugs/)

### 5.2 Connecticut (Leading State Enforcer)

- **May 2025:** AG Tong issued statewide notice that bulk compounding of semaglutide/tirzepatide is no longer permitted
- **2025:** Cease-and-desist letters sent to weight loss spas in Avon, Brookfield, and Danbury
- **2025:** Lawsuit filed against **Triggered Brand**, a distributor selling raw semaglutide and tirzepatide powders directly to consumers
- **Settlement reached** with Triggered Brand

**Source:** [CT AG Press Release](https://portal.ct.gov/ag/press-releases/2025-press-releases/attorney-general-tong-announces-new-developments-in-crackdown-on-bootleg-glp-1-weight-loss-drugs); [CT AG Triggered Brand](https://portal.ct.gov/ag/press-releases/2025-press-releases/attorney-general-tong-sues-glp-1-weight-loss-drug-distributor-triggered-brand)

### 5.3 Mississippi

AG issued consumer warnings urging use of only licensed and regulated sources for GLP-1 drugs, specifically flagging unapproved forms (sublingual drops, nasal sprays) as dangerous.

### 5.4 Pattern

State AGs have filled the enforcement gap left by slower FDA action. The "predictable enforcement wave" (per Stevens & Lee analysis) is led by consumer protection authorities rather than pharmacy boards, using UDAP (Unfair and Deceptive Acts and Practices) statutes.

**Source:** [Stevens & Lee](https://www.stevenslee.com/health-law-observer-blog/glp-1-weight-loss-drug-enforcement-in-2025-state-attorneys-general-step-into-a-growing-regulatory-gap/); [National Law Review](https://natlawreview.com/article/federal-and-state-agencies-ramp-scrutiny-glp-1-drug-market)

## 7. Implications for a Peptide Verification Service

### 7.1 Market Validation

The GLP-1 compounding crisis provides **overwhelming market validation** for a peptide verification service:

1. **Demonstrated quality failures:** 7-14% purity in a $100B+ drug class proves the problem is real and measurable
2. **Consumer harm is documented:** 900+ FAERS reports, 17 deaths, widespread hospitalizations
3. **Price-driven demand persists:** 75-90% cost savings ensure consumers will continue seeking compounded/alternative sources regardless of enforcement
4. **Enforcement creates information asymmetry:** As legitimate compounders exit and illegitimate ones remain, consumers have less ability to distinguish quality

### 7.2 Service Design Implications

- **Salt form detection is critical:** Semaglutide sodium/acetate vs semaglutide base is a key differentiator that affects safety and legality
- **Endotoxin testing matters:** All JMIR samples contained endotoxin; this is a testable, actionable quality marker
- **Content accuracy (not just purity):** Products exceeded labeled amounts by 29-39%, creating overdose risk even if the peptide itself is genuine
- **Certificate of Analysis (CoA) verification:** Claimed 99% purity vs 7-14% actual means vendor CoAs are unreliable and third-party testing is essential

### 7.3 Regulatory Positioning

A peptide verification service should position itself as:
- **Pro-consumer safety** (aligned with FDA, AG, and manufacturer interests)
- **Not facilitating illegal compounding** (verification of products consumers already have)
- **Complementary to enforcement** (providing data that regulators lack, since compounders don't report adverse events)
- **Evidence-based** (citing the JMIR study, FAERS data, and state AG findings)

### 7.4 Risk Factors

- Regulatory ambiguity: legal status of compounding continues to shift
- Potential liability if verification is interpreted as endorsement
- Need for clear disclaimers distinguishing testing from medical advice
- Some tested products may be outright illegal (post-shortage compounded copies)
