## Executive Summary

Oral peptide delivery has crossed from theoretical to commercial reality. Three milestone events define the current landscape:

1. **Oral Wegovy (semaglutide 25 mg)** — FDA-approved December 2025, launched January 2026. First oral GLP-1 for obesity. ~16.6% weight loss matching injectable Wegovy.
2. **ICOTYDE (icotrokinra)** — FDA-approved March 18, 2026. First oral peptide targeting IL-23 receptor for psoriasis. ~65% clear/almost-clear skin at 16 weeks.
3. **Orforglipron (Eli Lilly)** — Small-molecule oral GLP-1 in Phase 3, FDA action expected Q2 2026. ~12.4% weight loss at highest dose with NO food/water timing restrictions.

The oral peptide market is valued at $2.5-9.4 billion (2025) growing at 16.3% CAGR through 2034. Consumer preference for oral over injectable is a dominant market driver, with needle phobia and injection fatigue cited as primary barriers to peptide therapy initiation.

For peptide-checker: oral formulations introduce new quality verification challenges (acid stability, enhancer presence, bioavailability claims) that represent both a testing opportunity and a consumer education mandate.

## 1. Oral Semaglutide (Rybelsus / Oral Wegovy)

### 1.1 How Oral Semaglutide Works: The SNAC System

Oral semaglutide is co-formulated with **SNAC (sodium N-[8-(2-hydroxybenzoyl)amino] caprylate)**, a permeation enhancer originally developed by Emisphere Technologies (Eligen technology). Each Rybelsus tablet contains 7-14 mg semaglutide co-formulated with ~400 mg SNAC.

**SNAC mechanism of action (four-part process):**

1. **Local pH buffering** — SNAC neutralizes gastric acid in the immediate vicinity of the dissolving tablet, raising local pH. This reduces conversion of pepsinogen to pepsin, protecting semaglutide from enzymatic degradation.
2. **Monomerization** — SNAC changes local solution polarity, weakening hydrophobic interactions that cause semaglutide to form oligomers. Monomeric semaglutide is more readily absorbed.
3. **Membrane fluidization** — SNAC incorporates into the lipid bilayer of gastric epithelial cells, inducing a solid-to-fluid structural transition that increases transcellular permeability.
4. **Concentrated local absorption** — Absorption occurs in the stomach (not intestine), confined to a small area near the tablet surface. This requires direct co-formulation — SNAC and semaglutide cannot be taken separately.

All SNAC effects are **transient, size-dependent, and reversible**. SNAC has GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe) status from the FDA.

**2025 mechanistic update:** A Nature Communications study confirmed that SNAC-induced membrane defects are the primary driver of transcellular absorption for polar peptides, providing the first detailed molecular-level explanation of the process.

**2026 safety signal:** A February 2026 study found SNAC significantly alters gut microbiota composition, depleting primary fermenters and reducing fecal butyrate concentrations. This is an emerging area requiring monitoring.

### 1.2 Bioavailability: The 1% Reality

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Oral bioavailability (standard dosing) | ~0.8-1.0% |
| Oral bioavailability (optimal conditions) | ~1.4% (120-min post-dose fast) |
| Relative to subcutaneous injection | ~0.9% |
| Effect of water volume | Decreases with volumes >240 mL |
| Effect of food | Dramatically reduces absorption |

**Key implication:** Oral semaglutide requires ~10-20x the dose of injectable to achieve equivalent systemic exposure. Rybelsus 14 mg oral delivers comparable exposure to ~0.5-1.0 mg injectable semaglutide weekly.

**Dosing constraints:**
- Take on empty stomach with no more than 4 oz (120 mL) water
- Wait at least 30 minutes before eating, drinking, or taking other medications
- These constraints exist because of SNAC's mechanism — food and excess water dilute the concentrated local absorption zone

## 2. Oral BPC-157

### 2.1 BPC-157 Arginate vs. Acetate: The Acid Survival Advantage

**Patent WO2014142764A1** (Priority: March 13, 2013; Published: September 18, 2014)
- **Inventor:** Rudolf Rucman
- **Assignee:** Diagen d.o.o. (Croatia)
- **Covers:** Novel stable pentadecapeptide (bepecin) salts formed with basic amino acids (arginine, lysine, ornithine) in 1:1, 1:2, or 1:3 molar ratios

**Peptide sequence:** Gly-Glu-Pro-Pro-Pro-Gly-Lys-Pro-Ala-Asp-Asp-Ala-Gly-Leu-Val

**Preferred salt forms:**
- Bepecin di-L-arginine salt (Arg-BPC) — most favorable
- Bepecin L-lysine salt (Lys-BPC)
- Bepecin L-ornithine salt (Orn-BPC)

#### Acid Survival Data (Simulated Gastric Juice, pH 3.0)

| Salt Form | 2 hours | 3 hours | 5 hours |
|-----------|---------|---------|---------|
| **BPC-157 arginate** | **~93.6%** | **~90.0%** | **~84.9%** |
| BPC-157 acetate | ~7.8% | ~2.5% | Negligible |
| BPC-157 sodium | Rapid degradation | Rapid degradation | Negligible |

The arginate salt provides a **12x improvement** in acid survival at 2 hours compared to acetate. This is the foundational data supporting oral BPC-157 arginate products.

**Why arginine works:** The di-arginine salt creates ionic interactions that stabilize the peptide's tertiary structure, protecting vulnerable amide bonds from acid-catalyzed hydrolysis and pepsin cleavage. The basic amino acids also provide local pH buffering.

### 2.3 Evidence for Oral BPC-157 Efficacy

#### What the evidence shows:

**For GI applications (oral delivery = direct local action):**
- Oral BPC-157 acts directly on the gastrointestinal tract before systemic absorption
- Strong preclinical evidence for gastric ulcer healing, intestinal inflammation reduction, and gut barrier repair
- BPC-157 is one of very few peptides naturally stable enough for some oral absorption
- Oral route may be **preferred** for GI-targeted applications

**For systemic/musculoskeletal applications:**
- Preclinical evidence for tendon healing, muscle repair, bone healing, ligament regeneration, neural protection is **almost entirely based on injectable protocols**
- Oral BPC-157 may provide some systemic anti-inflammatory benefit, but bioavailability for non-GI targets is unestablished
- Injectable remains the clinical preference for localized musculoskeletal injuries
- No published human pharmacokinetic data for oral BPC-157 systemic bioavailability

#### Evidence quality assessment:

| Factor | Status |
|--------|--------|
| Human RCTs for oral BPC-157 | **None published** |
| Human PK data (oral bioavailability) | **Not established** |
| Preclinical studies (total) | 35 preclinical + 1 clinical (systematic review) |
| Safety/adverse event data in humans | **No studies assessed this** |
| Mechanistic rationale for oral GI effects | Strong (direct local action) |
| Mechanistic rationale for oral systemic effects | Weak (unknown bioavailability) |

**Bottom line:** Oral BPC-157 arginate is well-supported for GI applications by mechanism and preclinical data, but claims of systemic efficacy from oral dosing are not supported by human evidence.

## 3. Oral Peptide Delivery Technologies

### 3.1 Technology Landscape

#### 3.1.1 SNAC (Sodium N-[8-(2-hydroxybenzoyl)amino] caprylate)

- **Status:** Commercially proven (Rybelsus, oral Wegovy)
- **Mechanism:** Local pH buffering + membrane fluidization + monomerization
- **Site:** Gastric (stomach) absorption
- **Bioavailability achieved:** ~0.8-1.4%
- **Advantages:** GRAS status, FDA-approved track record, well-characterized safety
- **Limitations:** Strict dosing requirements (fasting, water volume), gut microbiome effects, still <2% bioavailability
- **Developer:** Emisphere Technologies (Eligen platform), now Novo Nordisk

#### 3.1.2 Sodium Caprate (C10) and Medium-Chain Fatty Acids

- **Status:** Phase 2 clinical validation (Merck's MK-0616, oral PCSK9 inhibitor)
- **Mechanism:** Transiently opens tight junctions between epithelial cells (paracellular transport)
- **Site:** Intestinal absorption
- **Advantages:** FDA-approved food additive, well-tolerated, combinable with SNAC
- **Limitations:** Paracellular route limits molecule size, requires enteric coating to reach intestine
- **Key development:** SNAC + C10 combination showed higher bioavailability than either alone in preclinical studies

#### 3.1.3 Enteric Coatings

- **Status:** Commercially available technology, widely used
- **Mechanism:** pH-sensitive polymers dissolve only in intestinal pH (>5.5-7.0), protecting cargo from gastric acid
- **Common polymers:** Eudragit L100, Eudragit S100, HPMC phthalate (HP-55)
- **Advantages:** Well-established, inexpensive, regulatory-friendly
- **Limitations:** Protects from acid but does NOT enhance absorption; must be combined with permeation enhancers or other technologies
- **Used in:** Most oral BPC-157 capsule products (acid-resistant capsule shells)

#### 3.1.4 Cell-Penetrating Peptides (CPPs)

- **Status:** Preclinical / early clinical
- **Mechanism:** Short, positively charged amino acid sequences that cross cell membranes via direct translocation or endocytosis
- **Advantages:** Can carry large peptide cargoes across epithelial barriers
- **Limitations:** Stability in GI tract, potential immunogenicity, cost of synthesis
- **Key data:** CPP-functionalized liposomes showed substantially increased oral bioavailability vs. unfunctionalized liposomes (preclinical)

#### 3.1.5 Nanoparticle Encapsulation

- **Status:** Preclinical / early clinical
- **Types:** Lipid nanoparticles (LNPs), polymeric nanoparticles (PLGA, chitosan), solid lipid nanoparticles
- **Mechanism:** Encapsulation protects peptide from enzymatic degradation; surface modifications target specific GI regions
- **Advantages:** Can combine protection + targeting + sustained release
- **Limitations:** Scale-up challenges, batch-to-batch variability, storage stability, high manufacturing cost
- **Future potential:** High — if manufacturing challenges are solved

#### 3.1.6 Mucoadhesive Systems

- **Status:** Preclinical / formulation stage
- **Key material:** Chitosan and derivatives (trimethyl chitosan, carboxymethyl chitosan, thiolated chitosan)
- **Mechanism:** Thiolated chitosan forms disulfide bonds with cysteine groups in mucus glycoproteins, prolonging GI residence time and protecting peptide cargo from enzymatic digestion
- **Advantages:** Biodegradable, biocompatible, enhances both adhesion and permeation
- **Limitations:** Variable mucus turnover rates, limited to mucosal surface contact

#### 3.1.7 Small-Molecule GLP-1 Agonists (Non-Peptide Oral)

- **Status:** Phase 3 / near-approval (orforglipron)
- **Mechanism:** Not a peptide at all — small molecule that mimics GLP-1 peptide at the receptor
- **Developer:** Eli Lilly (orforglipron)
- **Advantages:** No permeation enhancer needed, no food/water timing restrictions, conventional oral bioavailability
- **Key data:** Orforglipron 36 mg achieved 12.4% weight loss (ATTAIN-1), FDA submission filed, action expected Q2 2026
- **Significance:** Bypasses the oral peptide delivery problem entirely by designing a non-peptide agonist

## 5. Quality and Testing Challenges

### 5.1 Verifying Oral Peptide Bioavailability

**The fundamental challenge:** Oral peptide bioavailability is extremely low (typically <2%), making verification difficult and claims easy to exaggerate.

**Testing approaches:**
1. **In vitro acid stability testing** — Incubation in simulated gastric fluid (SGF) at pH 1.2-3.0 with pepsin, measuring peptide survival by HPLC/MS over time
2. **In vitro permeability assays** — Caco-2 cell monolayers or MDCK cells to measure apparent permeability (Papp)
3. **Dissolution testing** — Standard USP dissolution testing adapted for peptide formulations
4. **In vivo pharmacokinetics** — Gold standard but expensive; measures plasma peptide levels after oral dosing
5. **Bioequivalence studies** — Required for regulatory approval; compare oral AUC/Cmax to reference

**For supplements (non-FDA-regulated):**
- No requirement to demonstrate bioavailability
- Identity, purity, and potency testing is the practical standard
- Acid survival testing (as in WO2014142764A1) is informative but not required
- Third-party certificates of analysis (CoA) are the consumer-accessible verification

### 5.2 Testing for SNAC or Other Enhancers

| Enhancer | Detection Method | Why It Matters |
|----------|-----------------|----------------|
| SNAC | HPLC-UV/MS (characteristic UV absorption from salicylate moiety) | Confirms oral semaglutide authenticity |
| C10 (sodium caprate) | GC-MS or HPLC | Verify permeation enhancer in formulation |
| Chitosan | FTIR, ninhydrin assay | Confirm mucoadhesive coating presence |
| Enteric coating polymers | Dissolution testing at sequential pH values | Verify acid-resistant coating function |

### 5.3 Stability of Oral Peptide Formulations

**Key stability concerns:**
- **Temperature sensitivity** — Most peptide formulations require controlled storage (2-8C or room temperature <25C)
- **Moisture sensitivity** — Peptides degrade via hydrolysis; desiccants and moisture-barrier packaging essential
- **Light sensitivity** — UV exposure can cause oxidation of methionine and tryptophan residues
- **Shelf life** — Oral peptide supplements typically claim 12-24 months; independent verification is rare

**Regulatory standards for pharma products:**
- ICH Q1A guidelines for long-term stability (25C/60% RH, 40C/75% RH accelerated)
- Must demonstrate identity, purity, potency, and dissolution stability throughout shelf life
- GMP compliance for manufacturing facilities

**For supplements:**
- No mandated stability testing under DSHEA
- Responsible manufacturers conduct stability testing voluntarily
- ProHealth's triple-testing approach is an industry leader but not typical

### 5.4 Consumer Education Needs

**Critical dosing information for oral peptides:**

| Factor | Oral Semaglutide (Rybelsus/Wegovy pill) | Oral BPC-157 |
|--------|----------------------------------------|--------------|
| Fasting requirement | **30 min before food** | Ideally on empty stomach |
| Water volume | No more than 4 oz (120 mL) | Standard water OK |
| Time of day | Morning recommended | No strong preference |
| Food interactions | Food dramatically reduces absorption | Food may reduce absorption |
| Other medications | Wait 30 min after dose | Separate from other supplements |
| Alcohol interaction | Limited data; caution advised | No data |
| Split dosing | Not recommended | Some practitioners recommend split dosing |

**Common consumer misconceptions:**
1. "Oral peptides are as effective as injectable for all applications" — **False** for most peptides
2. "Higher dose oral = same as lower dose injectable" — Only true if bioavailability is characterized
3. "Enteric coating = guaranteed peptide delivery" — Enteric coating protects from acid but doesn't enhance absorption
4. "BPC-157 arginate is fully absorbed orally" — No human PK data supports this claim
5. "All oral BPC-157 products contain arginate salt" — Some use cheaper acetate; label verification needed

### 6.2 Testing Recommendations for Oral Products

**Tier 1 — Minimum (Identity + Purity):**
- Peptide identity confirmation (mass spectrometry)
- Purity by HPLC (>95% target)
- Salt form verification (arginate vs. acetate for BPC-157)
- Heavy metals and microbial contamination

**Tier 2 — Enhanced (+ Stability):**
- Acid survival testing (SGF at pH 1.2 and 3.0, with pepsin)
- Dissolution testing (capsule disintegration + peptide release)
- Moisture content analysis
- Certificate of Analysis cross-verification

**Tier 3 — Premium (+ Bioavailability Indicators):**
- In vitro permeability assay (Caco-2)
- Enhancer presence/absence verification (if claimed)
- Accelerated stability testing (40C/75% RH for 3 months)
- Comparison to reference standard

**Specific oral BPC-157 testing protocol:**
1. Confirm peptide identity (MS, sequence confirmation)
2. Verify salt form is arginate (not acetate relabeled)
3. Test acid survival at pH 3.0 over 2 hours (should retain >90% if true arginate)
4. Verify capsule acid resistance (if enteric-coated)
5. Check potency matches label claim (500 mcg or whatever stated)
