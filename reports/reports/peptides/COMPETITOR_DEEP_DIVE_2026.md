## 1. ConsumerLab.com

### 1.1 Company Profile

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Founded** | 1999 |
| **Founder** | Dr. Tod Cooperman, MD (Boston University School of Medicine, 1987; BS Medical Sciences with Economics minor) |
| **Headquarters** | Scarsdale, New York |
| **Team size** | ~9 employees (per ZoomInfo/Apollo) |
| **Legal entity** | ConsumerLab.com, LLC (privately held) |
| **Other ventures** | Dr. Cooperman also founded PharmacyChecker.com (international pharmacy price comparison) |

**Founder credibility:** Cooperman has testified as an expert witness before both the U.S. House and Senate on supplement quality and regulation. He is regularly invited to speak at the National Institutes of Health. VP Mark Anderson holds a PhD in pharmacology/toxicology with 10 peer-reviewed publications. The team is science-first, not marketing-first.

### 1.2 Revenue and Business Model

**Revenue estimate:** $10-$100M annually (per IncFact/Owler analysis of company filings). Given team size of 9 and a subscription-heavy model, the realistic range is likely $5-15M. This is a remarkably capital-efficient business.

**Revenue streams (in order of significance):**

1. **Consumer subscriptions (primary)** -- $69/year (1-year) or $105/2-years ($4.38/month). Previously $47/year; price has increased significantly, suggesting strong retention and pricing power.
2. **CL Seal of Approval licensing** -- Brands that pass testing can pay to license the CL Seal for product labels and marketing. Annual relicensing required (must pass retesting every 12 months). Fees not publicly disclosed, but at least $4,000+ per product for testing alone.
3. **Quality Certification Program fees** -- Separate from editorial reviews; brands voluntarily submit products for testing and pay the testing fee. Results are proprietary to the brand unless they pass and opt in to the seal program.
4. **Content licensing/republication** -- Media outlets, healthcare organizations, and retailers license CL data for their own publications.
5. **Advertising** -- Present but secondary; does not appear to dominate revenue.
6. **Survey reports** -- Proprietary survey data on supplement usage sold to industry.

**Key insight:** ConsumerLab runs a 9-person operation on what is likely $5-15M in annual revenue. That is an extraordinary margin profile. The subscription model provides predictable recurring revenue. The seal/certification program adds high-margin B2B revenue without increasing headcount.

### 1.3 Website Traffic and Reach

- ~1.1M total monthly visits (SimilarWeb, 2025)
- Ranked #17,647 in the US
- 33.74% direct traffic, 20.61% from Google
- Bounce rate: 49.88%
- The high direct traffic percentage indicates strong brand awareness and returning subscribers

### 1.4 Testing Scale

- **7,000+ products tested** since 1999
- **1,000+ brands** covered
- Covers nearly every type of popular supplement for adults, children, and pets, plus healthy foods, beverages, and health protection devices

### 1.5 Methodology

ConsumerLab's methodology is central to its value proposition:

1. **Retail purchase:** Products are purchased from public storefronts and online retailers, not supplied by manufacturers. This eliminates "golden sample" bias.
2. **Independent lab testing:** CL contracts testing to outside FDA-registered laboratories. It does NOT operate its own lab. Testing methods include HPLC for potency and ICP-MS for heavy metals.
3. **Full data publication:** Unlike pass/fail systems, CL publishes the actual measured values, allowing consumers to compare products directly.
4. **Proprietary review process:** Results are compiled into comprehensive Product Reviews covering identity, potency, purity (heavy metals, contaminants), bioavailability, and label accuracy.
5. **Annual retesting:** Products must pass retesting annually to maintain the CL Seal.

### 1.6 Content Strategy

CL reviews are dense, long-form, and expert-written. They include:

- **Product Reviews** -- Category-level deep dives testing 20-40 products (e.g., "Multivitamins Review")
- **CL Answers** -- Q&A format addressing specific consumer questions
- **Recalls and Warnings** -- Breaking news on supplement safety issues
- **Newsletter** -- Regular email digest for subscribers
- **Top Picks** -- Editorially selected best products within each category

**Criticism of content:** Reviews are "extremely extensive and a bit too wordy." The website "looks as though it was designed in 1990." Navigation to actual test data requires scrolling through walls of text. Poor mobile experience. The information architecture is the weakest point -- the data is excellent but the UX is not.

### 1.7 Legal and Regulatory Handling

CL operates in the same regulatory gray area as peptide verification:

- **DSHEA (1994)** means supplements are not pre-approved by FDA, creating the market need CL fills
- CL does not make efficacy claims -- it only reports on identity, purity, and label accuracy
- The CL Seal is a voluntary quality mark, not a regulatory certification
- CL has been cited in congressional hearings and FDA proceedings, giving it quasi-regulatory authority
- No known major lawsuits against CL for its testing/rating activities (a critical precedent for peptide-checker)

### 1.8 Trust Factors

What makes ConsumerLab trusted:

1. **25+ years of consistent operation** -- Longevity is the ultimate trust signal
2. **Independence from brands** -- Revenue comes primarily from consumers, not the industry being evaluated
3. **Retail purchase model** -- Cannot be gamed by manufacturers sending "golden samples"
4. **Congressional testimony** -- Founder's expert witness status confers institutional credibility
5. **Full data transparency** -- Publishing actual values, not just pass/fail
6. **No venture capital** -- No external pressure to compromise independence for growth

### 1.9 Weaknesses and Criticisms

1. **Paywall limits impact.** Most data is subscription-only, reducing public benefit and virality.
2. **No lab identity disclosure.** CL does not reveal which labs conduct its testing, limiting reproducibility.
3. **Brand selection bias.** Testing focuses on mass-market brands (CVS, Sam's Club) rather than premium/niche products that health-conscious consumers actually use.
4. **UX is terrible.** Website looks dated. Long-form reviews are hard to navigate. Mobile experience is poor.
5. **Inconsistent ratings.** Products previously rated "top picks" have later been downgraded without clear explanation of criteria changes.
6. **Certification program creates soft conflicts.** Brands pay for testing and seal licensing, creating a revenue stream that depends on maintaining brand relationships.
7. **Small team limits coverage.** 9 employees cannot cover the full supplement market, let alone emerging categories like peptides.

### 1.10 Peptide Coverage

ConsumerLab has published content on BPC-157 and TB-500 (CL Answers format), noting that "neither compound can be legally sold in the U.S. as an ingredient in dietary supplements and neither is approved as a drug." However, **CL has not conducted product testing of peptides.** This is a deliberate decision -- the regulatory ambiguity of research peptides likely makes them too risky for CL's methodology, which depends on products being commercially available supplements.

**This is the gap peptide-checker fills.**

### 2.2 Funding History

| Round | Year | Amount | Lead Investors |
|-------|------|--------|----------------|

| Seed | 2015 | ~$2M | Rock Health, Floodgate |

The 2022 Wefunder campaign is a significant data point -- it signals that traditional VC funding had dried up and the company was seeking alternative capital from its community.

### 2.3 Business Model Evolution (Cautionary Tale)

Labdoor's business model has pivoted multiple times, and each pivot reveals important lessons:

**Phase 1: Free ratings + affiliate links (2013-2016)**
- Published free supplement grades (A-F scale)
- Made money when users clicked through to buy products via affiliate links
- Problem: "Only single-digit percentages of consumers who look at the Labdoor site actually buy from it"
- Lesson: **Information-seekers are browsers, not buyers. Affiliate conversion on research content is terrible.**

**Phase 2: "Verified commerce" / direct sales (2016-2018)**
- Pivoted to selling tested products directly on the platform
- Claimed 15M+ shoppers since November 2013
- All reports made free, no sign-up required
- Problem: Competing with Amazon on commerce is suicide for a 15-person startup
- Lesson: **Being the testing authority AND the store creates trust conflicts and operational complexity.**

**Phase 3: Cryptocurrency token (2018)**
- Launched TEST token via ICO in June 2018
- Vision: decentralized product testing bounties where anyone could fund testing of any product
- TEST Foundation created to manage the protocol
- Problem: The crypto market crashed in late 2018. The token appears to have gone nowhere.
- Lesson: **Do not chase tech hype cycles. A supplement testing company has no business launching a cryptocurrency.**

**Phase 4: Certification services + enterprise (2019-present)**
- Shifted focus to B2B vendor certification
- Brands pay for testing; if they pass, Labdoor drives sales through certification badges and retailer referrals
- "Labdoor Certified" seal offered to passing products
- This is essentially the ConsumerLab seal program model
- Lesson: **B2B certification is the sustainable revenue model for testing platforms.**

**Phase 5: Low-activity maintenance mode (2023-present)**
- Team down to ~5 employees
- Website still operational but updates appear less frequent
- Domain renewed through 2026
- No significant product launches or media coverage

### 2.4 Grading Methodology

Labdoor tests for:
- **Label accuracy** -- Does the product contain what it claims?
- **Product purity** -- Contaminants, heavy metals, banned substances
- **Nutritional value** -- Micronutrient content and quality
- **Ingredient safety** -- Presence of harmful additives
- **Projected efficacy** -- Based on dosage vs. clinical literature

Results are presented as letter grades (A-F) with numerical scores. Products are ranked within categories.

### 2.5 What Worked

1. **Free access built a massive audience** -- 15M+ visitors/shoppers is significant for a niche product
2. **Y Combinator pedigree** -- Opened doors for funding and media coverage
3. **Simple grading system** -- A-F letter grades are immediately comprehensible
4. **Consumer advocacy positioning** -- "We test what you actually buy" resonated

### 2.6 What Failed

1. **Affiliate revenue is not sustainable** for an information/testing platform
2. **The pivot to commerce was a strategic error** -- destroyed the independence narrative
3. **The crypto pivot was a desperation move** that damaged credibility
4. **Venture capital created growth pressure** incompatible with the slow, methodical trust-building that testing platforms require
5. **No facility audits or GMP verification** -- testing products is necessary but not sufficient
6. **Team attrition from 15-20 to 5** indicates a company in managed decline

### 2.7 Current Status

Labdoor appears to be operational but in maintenance mode. The website is live, domain is renewed, and some product data is still referenced in 2025-2026 publications. However, with only 5 employees and no recent funding, it is not actively growing or expanding its testing coverage. The company is a walking cautionary tale about what happens when you take VC money for a trust-based business.

### 2.8 Lessons for Peptide-Checker

| Labdoor Mistake | Peptide-Checker Response |
|----------------|-------------------------|
| Took VC money, creating growth pressure | Bootstrap. Stay solo/lean. Revenue-first. |
| Pivoted business model 5 times | Pick subscription + certification from day one. Stay disciplined. |
| Free access killed monetization | Freemium with clear premium value (COA analyzer, alerts, detailed reports) |
| Launched a cryptocurrency | Never do this. |
| Tried to be the store AND the rater | Never sell peptides. Independence is the product. |
| No facility audits | Build toward supply chain verification, not just product testing |

## 3. Clean Label Project

### 3.1 Company Profile

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Type** | 501(c)(3) nonprofit |
| **Focus** | Heavy metals, pesticides, plasticizers, and industrial contaminants in consumer products |
| **Revenue (2023)** | $720,193 |
| **Testing scope** | Proteins, baby food, pet food, supplements |
| **Key figure** | Jaclyn Bowen, Executive Director |

### 3.2 Business Model

Clean Label Project operates as a nonprofit but functions like a certification business:

1. **Product testing** -- Purchases products at retail, tests at ISO-accredited partner labs
2. **Certification program** -- Brands that pass testing can earn "Clean Label Project Certified" mark
3. **Brand certification fees** -- Companies pay to acquire and maintain certification (specific pricing not disclosed publicly)
4. **Donations** -- Individual donors (average $25 donation); Amazon Smiles (~few thousand $/month)
5. **No disclosed donors** -- As a 501(c)(3), not required to disclose donor identity

### 3.3 Testing Methodology

- Samples purchased at retail (consumer chain of custody model)
- Tested at ISO-accredited analytical chemistry partner labs
- Tests include:
  - Heavy metals (arsenic, cadmium, lead, mercury)
  - 300+ pesticides including glyphosate
  - Plasticizers (BPA, BPS, phthalates)
  - Other chemicals of concern
- Results compared against California Proposition 65 list
- Testing takes up to 3 weeks

### 3.4 Credibility and Criticisms

**Serious credibility problems:**

1. **Pharmavite lawsuit (2020):** Pharmavite (Nature Made brand) sued CLP, alleging it operates a "protection racket" -- that CLP threatened Pharmavite with legal action based on test results unless they paid a **$1.9 million settlement**. Pharmavite refused and sued.

2. **Natural Products Association criticism:** The NPA PAC accused CLP of using "misinformation campaigns to scare consumers away from products" and then profiting from certification fees to those same companies.

3. **Methodology opacity:** Industry stakeholders allege testing methodology is murky and studies are not peer-reviewed.

4. **No conflict of interest policy:** As of recent reviews, CLP has no published conflict of interest or whistleblower policy.

5. **Financial instability:** Liabilities-to-assets ratio of 400.85%, suggesting the organization may be over-leveraged.

6. **Dual role conflict:** Evaluating products AND selling certifications to those same product makers is a structural conflict of interest.

### 3.5 Lessons for Peptide-Checker

**What to learn:**
- B2B certification is high-margin ($1.9M settlement demand shows the perceived value brands place on certification/reputation)
- Nonprofit structure can be strategically advantageous (tax benefits, perception of public interest)
- Consumer chain-of-custody sampling (buying at retail) is the gold standard for trust

**What to avoid:**
- CLP's perceived "pay-to-play" dynamic is toxic to credibility
- Lack of published methodology destroys trust among sophisticated consumers
- Testing without published data (unlike ConsumerLab's full disclosure) invites suspicion
- The entire CLP model is a cautionary tale about how certification revenue can corrupt the mission

## 4. Other Certification Programs

### 4.1 USP Verified

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Operator** | U.S. Pharmacopeia (international nonprofit, est. 1820) |
| **What it tests** | Ingredient identity, potency (+/-10%), heavy metals, microbials, residual solvents, pesticides |
| **Methodology** | Pharmaceutical-grade lab analysis + GMP compliance audits + supply chain audits |
| **Target** | General consumers, clinicians, pharmacists |
| **Adoption** | Less than 2% of supplements carry USP Verified mark |
| **Strengths** | Most pharmaceutically-aligned certification; strong regulatory recognition; 200+ year institutional credibility |
| **Weaknesses** | Extremely low adoption; high certification costs; no efficacy testing; voluntary participation creates selection bias |

**Relevance to peptide-checker:** USP standards are the gold standard for compounding pharmacies. As peptides move toward Category 1 legitimate compounding, USP compliance becomes a key quality signal. Peptide-checker should track and display USP compliance status for compounding pharmacy sources.

### 6.2 What Peptide-Checker Can Do That No One Else Does

1. **AI-powered COA verification** -- No existing platform in either supplements or peptides offers automated Certificate of Analysis analysis that detects fabricated documents, cross-references known lab formats, and compares claimed results against database averages.

2. **Multi-source vendor quality aggregation** -- Finnrick has its own data; Janoshik has its own data; community forums have scattered reports. No one aggregates across all sources into a unified vendor quality score.

4. **Storage/degradation calculator** -- Based on peptide-specific stability research (RQ-PEP-004), estimate remaining potency given storage conditions. No competitor offers this.

5. **Healthcare MCP integrations** -- Direct connections to ChEMBL (compound data), ClinicalTrials.gov (active studies), CMS Coverage (insurance status), ICD-10 (diagnosis codes), NPI (provider verification). No peptide platform offers this level of clinical context.

6. **Community batch testing coordination** -- Structured group-buying for testing, solving the "testing costs more than the product" problem. This is the credit union model applied to quality assurance.

## 7. Lessons and Anti-Patterns

### 7.1 What to Copy from ConsumerLab

1. **Subscription-first revenue model.** ConsumerLab's primary revenue comes from consumers, not the industry it evaluates. This is the single most important architectural decision for trust. Peptide-checker should ensure that consumer subscription revenue exceeds vendor certification revenue in the early years.

2. **Retail/third-party purchase methodology.** ConsumerLab buys products the same way consumers do. Peptide-checker should aggregate data from sources that do the same (Finnrick, Janoshik), and when coordinating community batch testing, ensure samples are purchased by consumers, not provided by vendors.

3. **Full data transparency.** Publishing actual measured values (not just pass/fail) is a key trust differentiator. Peptide-checker's vendor profiles should show all available test data, including potency numbers, purity percentages, and chromatograms where available.

4. **Annual retesting requirement.** The CL Seal requires annual retesting. Peptide-checker's vendor certification should require quarterly or monthly retesting (peptides have more batch-to-batch variation than supplements).

5. **Expert credibility signals.** Cooperman's MD and congressional testimony are massive trust signals. Peptide-checker should build advisory relationships with pharmacologists, analytical chemists, or physicians who can provide credibility (even informal advisory board).

6. **Capital efficiency.** 9 employees generating $5-15M in revenue is extraordinary. Peptide-checker should aim for a similarly lean operation: automate everything possible, keep the team tiny, and let the content compound over time.

7. **25 years of compounding trust.** The single most important lesson: trust compounds like interest. Starting now, even small, begins the clock.

### 7.2 What to Avoid from Labdoor

1. **Do not take venture capital.** Labdoor's VC funding created growth pressure incompatible with the slow trust-building that verification platforms require. The pivot from testing to commerce to crypto was driven by investor expectations, not user needs. Peptide-checker must be bootstrapped.

2. **Do not make all content free.** Labdoor's decision to make everything free killed monetization. "It's really hard to make money selling information, and Labdoor's data doesn't fit well behind a paywall" is a lesson, not a strategy. Peptide-checker's freemium model should give away enough to build trust, but reserve high-value tools (COA analyzer, detailed reports, alerts) for paying subscribers.

3. **Do not sell the products you rate.** Labdoor's pivot to "verified commerce" destroyed the independence narrative. Peptide-checker must NEVER sell peptides. The moat is independence, and any commerce activity poisons it permanently.

4. **Do not pivot to crypto.** Self-explanatory. The TEST token was a desperate move that damaged credibility and wasted resources.

5. **Do not try to be everything.** Labdoor pivoted 5 times in 10 years. Each pivot confused the brand and diluted trust. Peptide-checker should pick its model (freemium + certification) and execute for years without major pivots.

6. **Do not let team size balloon.** Labdoor went from 5 to ~20 and back to 5. Hire slowly. Automate ruthlessly. The ConsumerLab model (9 people, $10M+ revenue) is the target, not the Labdoor model (20 people, uncertain revenue).

### 7.3 What to Learn from Clean Label Project

1. **B2B certification is high-margin.** The fact that CLP allegedly demanded $1.9M from Pharmavite shows the perceived value of certification and reputation management in the testing space. Vendor certification at $199-$999/month is the most scalable revenue stream.

2. **Nonprofit structure has strategic value.** CLP's 501(c)(3) status provides tax benefits and a perception of public interest. Peptide-checker should consider whether a nonprofit parent entity (for testing/research) with a for-profit operating company (for software/subscriptions) would be advantageous.

3. **But the CLP model is fundamentally broken.** When your primary revenue comes from certifying the companies you evaluate, you are one bad lawsuit away from existential crisis. The Pharmavite case should haunt every decision about revenue model design.

4. **Publish your methodology.** CLP's initial refusal to disclose testing methods created lasting credibility damage. Peptide-checker should publish its aggregation methodology, scoring algorithm, and data sources from day one.

### 7.4 Revenue Diversification Strategy

Based on all three precedents, peptide-checker should target the following revenue mix by Year 2:

| Revenue Stream | Target % | Rationale |
|---------------|----------|-----------|
| Consumer subscriptions | 40-50% | Primary trust signal; recurring; ConsumerLab model |
| Vendor certification | 25-35% | High-margin; scalable; B2B recurring |
| Testing lab referral commissions | 10-15% | Zero COGS; aligns incentives (more testing = more data) |
| B2B API access | 5-10% | Long-term growth; telehealth/pharmacy integration |
| Affiliate (supplies/accessories) | <5% | Minor supplemental; BAC water, storage supplies only |

**Critical constraint:** Consumer subscription revenue must always exceed vendor certification revenue in dollar terms. This ensures that the platform's financial incentives are aligned with consumer interests, not vendor interests. This is the structural lesson from both Labdoor and Clean Label Project.

## 8. Tactical Recommendations

### 8.1 Brand Positioning

**Primary positioning:** "Consumer Reports for peptides"

This phrase is already in the business plan executive summary. It should be the elevator pitch, the tagline, and the mental model for every decision. It works because:

- Consumer Reports is universally understood as independent, rigorous, and consumer-first
- It immediately communicates the value proposition
- It sets expectations for both methodology (independent testing) and business model (consumer-funded)

### 8.5 Trust-Building Strategy (The Independence Moat)

Independence is the core moat. Every decision should be evaluated against: "Does this make us more or less independent?"

**Structural trust mechanisms:**
1. **Publish a conflict of interest policy on day one.** Finnrick doesn't have one. CLP doesn't have one. This is a free credibility win.
2. **Disclose all revenue sources annually.** Even approximate percentages. Transparency about money is the highest trust signal.
3. **Never accept vendor-supplied samples for rating purposes.** All data must come from third-party or consumer-purchased testing.
4. **Maintain editorial/certification wall.** The team members who write reviews should not be the same people managing vendor certification relationships.
5. **Open-source the scoring methodology.** Publish the algorithm that converts raw test data into vendor scores. Let anyone audit it.
6. **Community advisory board.** 3-5 active peptide community members who can publicly vouch for the platform's independence.
