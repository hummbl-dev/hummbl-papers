## 1. Introduction: Why Peptide Quality Matters

Peptides are short chains of amino acids -- the same building blocks that make up proteins in your body. In recent years, consumer interest in peptides has surged, driven by the popularity of GLP-1 weight-management drugs like semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound), as well as research peptides like BPC-157, TB-500, and ipamorelin.

But there is a serious quality problem in the peptide market, and the data paints a concerning picture.

### The Numbers

**Independent testing of online semaglutide products** found that samples claiming 99% purity actually contained just **7.7% to 14.4% of the labeled peptide content** -- with **endotoxin contamination detected in every sample tested** (Ashraf et al., *Journal of Medical Internet Research*, 2024).

**Testing of BPC-157 from 68 vendors** by Finnrick Analytics (450 samples, December 2024 through March 2026) found that approximately **60% of vendors received "D" (Poor) or "E" (Bad) quality ratings**. Only about 10% of vendors achieved the top "A" rating for consistent quality.

**A study published in *Drug Testing and Analysis*** found that **30% of online peptide products contained incorrect amino acid sequences**, and **65% exceeded endotoxin safety thresholds**.

**The FDA's adverse event database (FAERS)** documented over **900 adverse event reports** and **17 deaths** linked to compounded GLP-1 products as of late 2024, with dosing errors causing 5-20x overdoses as a primary driver of hospitalizations.

These are not abstract statistics. They represent real products purchased by real consumers from real vendors.

### Why This Guide Exists

This guide compiles publicly available testing data, peer-reviewed research, and regulatory information into one resource. Its purpose is simple: to help you make informed decisions by understanding what the evidence shows about peptide quality, how to evaluate what you are buying, and how to handle peptides safely.

Every factual claim in this guide is attributable to sources cited in our underlying research reports, including peer-reviewed studies, FDA data, independent laboratory results, and legal analyses. Where data is limited or uncertain, we say so.

## 2. Understanding Peptide Purity

### What Does "Purity" Actually Mean?

When a laboratory reports that a peptide is "98% pure," it means that 98% of the material detected in the sample is the target peptide. The remaining 2% consists of other substances -- impurities, degradation products, residual solvents, or unrelated compounds.

Purity is not the same as potency. A vial could contain a very pure peptide but in the wrong amount. Or it could contain a reasonably pure but completely wrong peptide. Purity tells you about what fraction of the sample is the intended compound -- it does not tell you how much is in the vial or confirm that the peptide is what the label says.

### How Is Purity Measured?

Two primary laboratory methods are used to assess peptide quality:

**HPLC (High-Performance Liquid Chromatography)** is the standard workhorse test. It separates the components of a sample based on their chemical properties, then measures how much of the total material corresponds to the target peptide. HPLC can detect impurities down to about 0.05-0.1% of total content. It costs $120-$800 per test at consumer-facing labs, with results typically available within 2-21 days.

What HPLC measures well: purity percentage, degradation products, and gross contamination.

What HPLC cannot do: confirm *which* peptide is present. Two peptides with similar chemical properties can produce similar HPLC results. This is why identity confirmation requires a second method.

**LC-MS (Liquid Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry)** combines the separation power of HPLC with the identification power of mass spectrometry. After separating components, it measures their exact molecular weight, which serves as a molecular fingerprint. LC-MS can confirm peptide identity with near-absolute certainty and detect impurities down to 0.01-0.1%.

What LC-MS adds: definitive identity confirmation (is this actually BPC-157, or something else?), molecular weight verification, and sequence information.

**The bottom line:** HPLC tells you how pure the sample is. LC-MS tells you what the sample is. A reliable quality assessment uses both.

### What's a Good Purity Level?

| Purity Level | What It Means | Typical Context |
|---|---|---|
| **>98%** | Research-grade; acceptable for most applications | Top-tier vendors; pharmaceutical intermediate standard |
| **95-98%** | Acceptable but may contain meaningful impurity levels | Mid-tier vendors; may indicate degradation or synthesis issues |
| **90-95%** | Significant impurities present | Below standard for most purposes |
| **<90%** | Substantial contamination or degradation | Quality failure; the product may not perform as expected |
| **7-14%** | The product is mostly *not* the labeled peptide | Found in online semaglutide products (JMIR, 2024) |

Pharmaceutical-grade peptide drugs (like Ozempic or Wegovy) are manufactured to >99% purity under strict FDA oversight. The gap between pharmaceutical-grade and gray-market products is the core of the quality problem.

### Common Contaminants and What They Mean

**Endotoxins** are fragments of bacterial cell walls. They cannot be removed by standard filtration and can cause fever, chills, and in severe cases, septic shock when injected. Independent testing found endotoxin contamination in approximately 8% of gray-market samples (Finnrick data), while a *Drug Testing and Analysis* study found 65% of online peptides exceeded safety thresholds. Every online semaglutide sample tested in the JMIR study contained detectable endotoxins (2.16-8.95 EU/mg).

**Incorrect sequences and mislabeling** were found in 30% of online peptide products (*Drug Testing and Analysis*). A product labeled as one peptide may contain a completely different peptide, a partial peptide, or no active ingredient at all.

**Residual solvents**, particularly trifluoroacetic acid (TFA), are byproducts of the manufacturing process. TFA can constitute up to 35% of the gross weight of a peptide product in TFA salt form (Erckes et al., *Pharmaceuticals*). This means a "5 mg" vial of TFA-salt peptide may contain only 3.5-4.0 mg of actual peptide.

**Degradation products** form when peptides break down due to heat, light, moisture, or time. These are chemically related to the original peptide but may have altered or no biological activity. They appear as additional peaks on an HPLC chromatogram.

**Heavy metals** (lead, mercury, arsenic, cadmium) have been reported in some peptide products but are less systematically studied in public datasets. Screening is available as an add-on from testing labs like Janoshik ($120).

## 3. How to Read a Certificate of Analysis (COA)

A Certificate of Analysis (COA) is a document from a testing laboratory that reports the results of quality testing on a specific batch of product. Reputable vendors provide COAs for each batch they sell. Understanding what makes a COA trustworthy -- and what makes one suspicious -- is one of the most practical skills a peptide consumer can develop.

### What Should a Real COA Include?

A credible COA should contain all of the following elements:

| # | Element | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **HPLC purity value** | A specific percentage (e.g., 98.4%) with an accompanying chromatogram image |
| 2 | **Mass spectrometry data** | Molecular weight confirmation showing the correct mass-to-charge ratios |
| 3 | **Unique batch/lot number** | An alphanumeric identifier matching the label on your vial |
| 4 | **Testing laboratory name** | A named, verifiable laboratory with identifiable contact information |
| 5 | **Analysis date** | The date testing was performed -- ideally within the past 3 months |
| 6 | **HPLC method details** | Column type, mobile phase, flow rate, and detector wavelength |
| 7 | **Chromatogram** | The actual graphical output of the HPLC run, showing peaks and baseline |
| 8 | **Peptide identity** | The full amino acid sequence or CAS number of the peptide |
| 9 | **Net peptide content** | The actual peptide mass versus gross weight, accounting for salt form and moisture |

### The 9-Point Checklist for Spotting Fake or Suspicious COAs

When reviewing a COA, check for these red flags:

**1. Round-number purity values.** A real HPLC test almost never returns exactly "99.00%." Laboratory instruments produce specific decimal values like 98.73% or 99.14%. A perfectly round purity number suggests the document was fabricated rather than generated from actual testing.

**2. Missing laboratory name.** Phrases like "tested by a certified laboratory" without naming the lab are a major warning sign. A legitimate lab has a name, address, and verification system.

**3. No chromatogram.** The chromatogram is the raw graphical output of the HPLC instrument. A COA that claims HPLC purity but does not include the chromatogram is like a medical report that claims test results but does not include the actual lab printout.

**4. Identical chromatograms across products.** If the COA for BPC-157, tirzepatide, and TB-500 all show suspiciously similar chromatogram shapes, the documents were likely fabricated. Every peptide produces a unique chromatographic profile.

**5. Chromatograms that look too clean.** Real HPLC chromatograms show baseline noise -- a subtle wavy line representing normal instrument variation. A perfectly smooth baseline with a single pristine peak may indicate a digitally created image rather than actual instrument output.

**6. Stale analysis dates.** Testing labs like Janoshik enforce a 3-month freshness policy on COAs. A COA dated more than 6 months ago may not reflect the current quality of the product, especially for reconstituted or improperly stored peptides.

**7. Batch numbers that don't match.** The lot number on the COA should match the lot number on your vial. If there is no lot number on either document, or they do not match, the COA may not correspond to your product.

**8. No mass spectrometry data.** HPLC tells you the purity but not the identity. A COA without MS data cannot confirm that the product is actually the peptide on the label.

**9. Unverifiable results.** Some labs (notably Janoshik) offer public verification portals where you can enter a unique key from the COA and confirm the results are real. If a vendor claims Janoshik testing but the verification key does not check out, the COA may be fabricated.

### Example: Good COA vs. Bad COA

**A strong COA** would include a specific purity value like "99.14%" with a full chromatogram showing baseline noise, clear peak integration marks, and labeled retention times. It would include ESI-MS data confirming the correct molecular weight, a unique batch number matching the vial label, the name of the testing laboratory (e.g., "Janoshik Analytical, Czech Republic"), a recent date, and method details listing the column type, mobile phase composition, and UV detection wavelength. A verification key linked to the lab's public portal would further confirm authenticity.

**A weak COA** might list "Purity: 99.00%" with no chromatogram or a suspiciously clean graphic. It might say "Analysis performed by certified laboratory" without naming the lab, omit mass spectrometry data entirely, show no batch number or a generic one like "BPC-001," have no analysis date or method details, and be a single-page PDF with no verification mechanism.

## 4. Vendor Quality: What the Data Shows

### The A-E Tier System

Finnrick Analytics, an independent testing organization based in Texas, has tested 450 BPC-157 samples from 68 vendors between December 2024 and March 2026. They rate vendors on a 0-10 scale based on purity, quantity accuracy, and batch documentation, then assign letter grades.

**Important caveats about Finnrick data:** Finnrick lacks ISO/IEC 17025 accreditation at the organizational level. They operate a vendor revenue model (paid programs from $279/month). A 15% potency discrepancy between their contracted labs has been documented. Their data is the largest publicly available dataset but is not the sole or definitive source of vendor quality information.

| Rating | Description | Vendor Count | What It Means |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A** (Great) | 7 vendors | ~10% | Consistently high purity (>98%), accurate dosing, complete documentation |
| **B** (Good) | ~12 vendors | ~18% | Generally reliable with minor inconsistencies |
| **C** (Okay) | ~8 vendors | ~12% | Mixed results -- some batches acceptable, others problematic |
| **D** (Poor) | ~15 vendors | ~22% | Significant quality issues; underdosing common |
| **E** (Bad) | ~26 vendors | ~38% | Severe quality failures, mislabeling, or contamination |

### The Key Takeaway

Approximately **60% of tested vendors** fall into the D or E categories, indicating significant quality problems. Only about **10% of vendors** consistently deliver A-level quality.

Quantity accuracy is also a concern. Finnrick data shows that 5% of samples deviate by more than **75%** from the labeled peptide content. That means a "5 mg" vial might contain as little as 1.25 mg or as much as 8.75 mg of actual peptide.

### How to Evaluate a Vendor Before Buying

**1. Cross-reference multiple data sources.** Check Finnrick vendor ratings, Janoshik public test results (public.janoshik.com), and community reports on forums like Reddit r/Peptides. No single source is definitive, but consistent quality signals across multiple sources are meaningful.

**2. Look for vendors with multiple test results.** A vendor with 2 tests may be rated A by chance. A vendor with 7 or more tests showing consistent results is a stronger signal. Several "tentative" A-rated vendors on Finnrick have fewer than 6 tests -- treat those ratings cautiously.

**3. Examine pricing relative to quality.** Independent testing shows that prices below approximately $25 per 5 mg vial almost universally correlate with quality problems for BPC-157. Quality peptide synthesis has a floor cost that makes extreme discounts structurally unsustainable without cutting corners. However, high price alone does not guarantee quality -- several D/E-rated vendors charge mid-range prices.

| Price Tier | Typical Range (5 mg) | Quality Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Budget | $10-25 | High risk -- frequently underdosed, poor or absent COAs |
| Mid-range | $35-50 | Variable -- some reliable vendors operate here |
| Premium | $50-60 | Generally reliable with comprehensive third-party testing |

**4. Demand lot-specific COAs.** Every vial should have a batch number that matches a COA containing an HPLC chromatogram, MS data, a named testing lab, and a recent analysis date. Vendors that refuse to provide lot-specific COAs should be treated as suspect regardless of price.

### Questions to Ask Your Vendor

1. "Can you provide a lot-specific COA for this batch, including HPLC chromatogram and MS identity confirmation?"
2. "Which third-party laboratory performed the testing, and can I verify the results independently?"
3. "What salt form is this peptide in (TFA, acetate, or arginine), and is the labeled weight gross weight or net peptide content?"
4. "What are your storage and shipping conditions? Is the product shipped cold?"
5. "How long has this batch been in storage, and what is the recommended shelf life?"

**A note on vendor name confusion:** Finnrick data reveals that some vendor names are similar or identical across different entities. For example, "Prime Peptides" appears as D-rated in Finnrick data, while a vendor of the same name achieved 99%+ purity on Janoshik testing -- these may be different companies. Always verify using the vendor's website, not just the name.

## 5. Storage and Handling

Peptides are more fragile than most consumers realize. Proper storage is one of the simplest and most impactful things you can do to protect your investment and safety. The core principle is straightforward: **lyophilized (powder) peptides are robust; reconstituted (liquid) peptides are fragile.**

### Lyophilized (Powder) Storage Guidelines

Lyophilized peptides are freeze-dried into a powder or cake form. In this state, with low residual moisture and a sealed vial, they are remarkably stable.

| Storage Temperature | Expected Stability | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| **-80C (ultra-cold freezer)** | 5+ years | Long-term archival storage |
| **-20C (standard freezer)** | 2-3 years | Recommended for any storage >6 months |
| **2-8C (refrigerator)** | 6-18 months | Acceptable for active use within months |
| **Room temperature (20-25C)** | Weeks to a few months | Not recommended for routine storage |
| **Above 30C** | Days to weeks | Rapid degradation; avoid |

**Key practices for powder storage:**

- Store in the back of your freezer (most stable temperature; avoid the door).
- Keep vials sealed with intact crimp caps. A compromised seal allows moisture in -- and moisture is more destructive than moderate temperature excursions for sealed lyophilized peptides.
- Include desiccant packets if storing multiple vials together.
- Allow vials to warm to room temperature (15-30 minutes) before opening. This prevents moisture from condensing on the cold powder surface, which accelerates degradation.

### Reconstituted (Liquid) Storage Guidelines

Once you add liquid to a lyophilized peptide, the clock starts ticking. All degradation pathways -- hydrolysis, oxidation, deamidation, aggregation -- accelerate in solution.

| Diluent | Storage Temp | Maximum Use Window |
|---|---|---|
| **Bacteriostatic water (BAC)** | 2-8C (refrigerator) | 4-6 weeks |
| **Sterile water** | 2-8C (refrigerator) | 7-14 days |
| **Any diluent** | Room temperature | Not recommended (days at most) |

**Bacteriostatic water versus sterile water:** Bacteriostatic water contains 0.9% benzyl alcohol, which inhibits bacterial growth. This preservative is what allows multi-dose use over 4-6 weeks. Sterile water has no preservative -- bacteria can grow in the solution and metabolize the peptide as a nutrient source. If you plan to use a vial over multiple doses, bacteriostatic water is strongly preferred.

**Reconstitution best practices:**

1. Inject the diluent slowly down the inside wall of the vial -- never squirt directly onto the powder.
2. Swirl the vial very gently to dissolve. Never shake. Shaking creates air-water interfaces that can denature (damage) peptides.
3. The solution should be clear and colorless after dissolving.
4. Label the vial with the peptide name, concentration, reconstitution date, and discard date.
5. Refrigerate immediately.
6. Wipe the rubber septum with an alcohol swab before each needle draw.

### How to Know If Your Peptide Has Degraded

After reconstitution, watch for these visual signs:

| Sign | What It Means | What to Do |
|---|---|---|
| **Clear and colorless** | Normal | Use as intended |
| **Slight cloudiness** | Possible early aggregation or cold precipitation | Allow 30 minutes at room temperature; if it clears, likely acceptable; if it persists, discard |
| **Persistent milky cloudiness** | Protein aggregation or bacterial contamination | Discard |
| **Yellow or brown tint** | Oxidation of amino acid residues (irreversible) | Discard |
| **Visible particles or "snow globe" effect** | Aggregation or precipitation | Discard |
| **Gel-like consistency** | Advanced aggregation | Discard |
| **Wet or clumped lyophilized powder** (before reconstitution) | Moisture has entered the vial through a compromised seal | Do not reconstitute -- likely degraded |
| **Discoloration of dry powder** (yellow, brown) | Oxidation or chemical degradation | Suspect degradation |

### The Freeze-Thaw Problem

Freezing a reconstituted peptide solution and then thawing it causes measurable damage. Ice crystals form at the air-liquid interface and physically disrupt peptide structure. Studies estimate 1-5% peptide content loss per freeze-thaw cycle depending on the peptide, with some peptides losing 15-30% bioactivity per cycle.

**One critical exception: semaglutide must never be frozen.** Freezing permanently destroys the formulation's stability. This applies to the prefilled injection pens (Ozempic, Wegovy). If a pen has been frozen, it should be discarded.

For other peptides, if you need to freeze reconstituted solutions, divide them into single-use portions (aliquots) before freezing. This way each portion is only thawed once.

### Amber Vials and Light Protection

Peptides degrade when exposed to light, particularly ultraviolet (UV) light. This is especially true for peptides containing tryptophan or histidine residues.

Pharmaceutical-grade amber glass blocks approximately 90% of UV-B radiation (the most damaging wavelength range) and 70% of UV-A radiation. In one study, 78% of peptide serums stored in clear glass lost 40% of their active content after 8 weeks under ambient indoor lighting, while the same formulation in amber glass retained 92% potency.

**Practical recommendation:** Store peptide vials in amber glass when possible. If your vials are clear glass, wrap them in aluminum foil. Keep all peptide vials in a dark location -- inside a refrigerator, in a drawer, or in a box. Even brief repeated light exposures during daily use accumulate over weeks.

## 7. The Regulatory Landscape

The legal and regulatory environment for peptides in the United States is changing rapidly. This section summarizes the current state as of March 2026 in terms consumers can understand.

### The FDA Category System

The FDA classifies peptide ingredients used by compounding pharmacies into categories:

- **Category 1** substances *can* be used by licensed compounding pharmacies to prepare prescription medications.
- **Category 2** substances *cannot* be compounded because the FDA determined they "raise significant safety concerns." This is an interim administrative designation, not a formal drug ban.

In September 2023, the FDA placed **19 popular peptides on the Category 2 list**, including BPC-157, TB-500, CJC-1295, ipamorelin, and others. This meant licensed compounding pharmacies could no longer legally prepare these peptides for patients.

In September 2024, **5 of those 19 peptides** were removed from Category 2 consideration after the nominators withdrew their submissions: AOD-9604, CJC-1295, Ipamorelin Acetate, Thymosin Alpha-1, and Selank Acetate.

### The HHS Reclassification Announcement

On **February 27, 2026**, HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. announced that approximately **14 of the 19** Category 2 peptides would be reclassified back to Category 1, restoring access through licensed compounding pharmacies with a physician's prescription.

**However -- and this is critical -- as of March 2026, no formal FDA rulemaking has been published.** The announcement was made during a podcast appearance (Joe Rogan Experience #2461), not through official regulatory channels. No Federal Register notice has been issued. No statute has been amended. No official list of the 14 peptides has been published.

**What this means practically:** Until the FDA formally updates the Category 2 list, the legal status of these peptides for compounding remains technically unchanged. The announcement signals intent, but implementation is pending.

### What's Legal, What's Gray Area, What's Illegal

**Legally available with a prescription:**
- FDA-approved peptide drugs: semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy), tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound), tesamorelin (Egrifta), and a small number of others
- Category 1 compounded peptides from licensed 503A/503B pharmacies with a valid prescription
- Cosmetic/topical peptides (GHK-Cu in skincare, Matrixyl, Argireline) available over the counter

**Gray area (legal risk):**
- "Research use only" peptides purchased for legitimate laboratory research are technically legal to buy. However, the gray-market supply chain is collapsing -- 7+ research peptide companies shut down in 2025, and Peptide Sciences, the largest vendor, voluntarily ceased operations on March 6, 2026. Self-administration of research peptides is not sanctioned by the FDA.

**Definitively illegal:**
- Selling peptides with therapeutic health claims without FDA approval
- Compounding Category 2 peptides for human use (as of March 2026)
- Compounding semaglutide or tirzepatide (shortage resolved; grace periods expired)
- Importing counterfeit or adulterated peptides for sale
- Marketing injectable peptides as "dietary supplements" (they do not qualify under DSHEA)

### What If the Reclassification Happens?

If formalized, Category 1 status would:
- Allow licensed compounding pharmacies to prepare these peptides with a valid prescription
- **Not** make peptides available over the counter
- **Not** constitute FDA approval of the peptides as drugs
- **Not** legalize the gray-market "research use only" sales model
- Still require a physician-patient relationship and prescription for each patient

The reclassification would shift the market toward licensed compounding pharmacies with pharmaceutical-grade quality assurance -- but consumers would still need a prescription.

### State-Level Enforcement Varies Widely

States differ significantly in how aggressively they enforce peptide regulations:

| Enforcement Level | States | Key Actions |
|---|---|---|
| Very High | Connecticut | Forced peptide seller to cease operations; statewide notice prohibiting GLP-1 compounding |
| High | New York, California, Massachusetts, Alabama | Active investigations; strict compounding standards; Alabama obtained a restraining order against a peptide seller (Nov 2025) |
| Moderate | Illinois, Colorado, Ohio, Pennsylvania | Follow federal framework with active oversight; Ohio pharmacies fined for violations |
| Low | Florida, Nevada, Arizona | Hub for peptide therapy clinics; broad telemedicine prescribing rules |

In February 2025, **38 state and territory attorneys general** signed a joint letter to the FDA demanding federal action against counterfeit GLP-1 drugs entering the U.S. from China, Turkey, India, and other countries -- an unprecedented coalition signaling that states viewed the problem as urgent.

### For Athletes: WADA Prohibition Is Separate

The World Anti-Doping Agency (WADA) prohibits most peptides in competition and out of competition, regardless of their FDA regulatory status. Even if the FDA reclassifies peptides to Category 1 and a physician writes a prescription, **WADA prohibition remains in effect.** Athletes would need a Therapeutic Use Exemption (TUE), which is extremely difficult to obtain for performance-related peptides.

Peptides prohibited by WADA include: BPC-157, TB-500, CJC-1295, ipamorelin, MK-677 (ibutamoren), AOD-9604, Melanotan II, Semax, Selank, KPV, and MOTS-C, among others.

## 8. GLP-1 Agonists: A Special Warning

GLP-1 receptor agonists -- particularly semaglutide (the active ingredient in Ozempic and Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound) -- deserve special attention because of the scale of the quality crisis in compounded and unregulated versions.

### The Compounded Semaglutide Purity Crisis

The JMIR study (Ashraf et al., 2024) is the most rigorous publicly available analysis of online semaglutide quality. Researchers purchased semaglutide from 6 online vendors selling without a prescription. Three vendors delivered products; three were e-commerce scams that never shipped anything.

Of the three products that arrived:

| Vendor | Claimed Purity | Actual Purity | Endotoxin Level | Peptide Content vs. Label |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| SemaSpace | 99% | **14.37%** | 8.95 EU/mg | +28.56% (overfilled) |
| USChemLabs | 99% | **8.97%** | <2.87 EU/mg | +33.6% (overfilled) |
| BiotechPeptides | 99% | **7.70%** | <2.16 EU/mg | +38.69% (overfilled) |

**Source:** Ashraf AR et al., "Multifactor Quality and Safety Analysis of Semaglutide Products Sold by Online Sellers Without a Prescription," *JMIR*, 2024;26:e65440. PMID: 39509151.

Every sample was classified as a "probable substandard and falsified product." The purity values mean that **85-92% of what was in each vial was not semaglutide.** The identity of the remaining material -- the majority of what consumers would inject -- is unknown.

The content overfilling (28-39% above labeled amounts) is an additional danger: even if the product contained the intended peptide, users would be injecting significantly more than they intended.

### What the FDA Data Shows

The FDA's adverse event reporting system (FAERS) documented over **900 adverse event reports** and **17 deaths** linked to compounded GLP-1 products as of late 2024. The FDA estimates approximately 10 deaths and 100 hospitalizations were linked specifically to dosing errors, with patients receiving 5-20 times the intended dose.

A pharmacovigilance study analyzing 81,078 GLP-1 reports in FAERS (published in *Expert Opinion on Drug Safety*, 2025) found dramatically elevated risk signals for compounded versus non-compounded formulations:

- Preparation errors: **48.92x higher** reporting odds
- Contamination: **19.00x higher** reporting odds
- Compounding/manufacturing issues: **8.51x higher** reporting odds

Adverse events specifically elevated in compounded GLP-1 reports included abdominal pain, diarrhea, nausea, suicidality, and cholecystitis.

**Source:** Expert Opinion on Drug Safety, 2025 (DOI: 10.1080/14740338.2025.2499670); FDA FAERS database.

### Additional Quality Risks with Compounded GLP-1s

**Wrong salt form:** Some compounded products used semaglutide sodium or semaglutide acetate instead of the semaglutide base used in FDA-approved products. Whether these alternative salt forms are pharmacokinetically equivalent is unknown.

**Dosing confusion:** Multi-dose vials create confusion between milligrams, milliliters, and "units," contributing to the dosing errors documented in FAERS. FDA-approved pens use fixed-dose delivery mechanisms specifically to prevent this problem.

**Sterility failures:** In August 2024, ProRx recalled over 15,000 vials of compounded semaglutide and tirzepatide due to lack of sterility assurance.

**Fraudulent products:** The FDA has identified compounded products with fake pharmacy names on labels and products from non-existent compounding facilities.

### The Enforcement Landscape

The FDA issued **55+ warning letters** in September 2025 to companies compounding or marketing GLP-1 products. Novo Nordisk and Eli Lilly have launched aggressive litigation campaigns against compounders and telehealth platforms. The semaglutide shortage was officially resolved on February 21, 2025, and enforcement grace periods for compounders expired in April-May 2025.

Despite enforcement, IQVIA data from October 2025 shows compounded semaglutide prescriptions **increased** after the shortage ended. Approximately 80% of compounded semaglutide prescriptions now include supplemental ingredients (e.g., B vitamins) as a legal strategy to argue the compounded product is not an "essential copy" of the branded drug -- a strategy of uncertain legal validity.

### How to Verify What You're Getting

If you are using a GLP-1 medication:

1. **FDA-approved products from licensed pharmacies are the safest option.** Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro, and Zepbound are manufactured to >99% purity under strict quality controls.

2. **If using a compounded product, verify the pharmacy.** Licensed 503A pharmacies should be registered with your state board of pharmacy. Licensed 503B outsourcing facilities should be registered with the FDA. You can check FDA registration at [fda.gov](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/human-drug-compounding).

3. **Ask for third-party testing.** A compounding pharmacy should be able to provide batch-specific testing results from an independent laboratory -- not just in-house testing.

4. **Be aware of price signals.** FDA-approved GLP-1 medications cost $1,300-$1,900 per month without insurance. Compounded versions typically cost $150-$500 per month. The 75-90% cost difference is the primary driver of consumer demand, but it also correlates with quality risk.

5. **Report adverse events.** If you experience unexpected side effects, report them to the FDA's MedWatch program and your healthcare provider.

## 9. Frequently Asked Questions

### Q1: Are peptides legal to buy?

It depends on the peptide and the context. FDA-approved peptide drugs (like semaglutide) are legal with a prescription from a licensed pharmacy. Cosmetic peptides (like GHK-Cu in skincare) are legal over the counter. "Research use only" peptides exist in a legal gray area -- purchasing for legitimate research is technically legal, but the gray market is collapsing due to FDA enforcement, DOJ prosecutions, and manufacturer litigation. Self-administration of research peptides is not sanctioned by the FDA.

### Q2: How do I know if my peptide is real?

The only definitive way to confirm peptide identity is laboratory testing with mass spectrometry (LC-MS). Visual inspection and HPLC purity testing are useful but cannot confirm identity. If you want to verify a specific vial, services like Janoshik Analytical ($120-$300) provide LC-MS identity confirmation. Short of lab testing, review the vendor's COA carefully using the 9-point checklist in Section 3, cross-reference the vendor against Finnrick and Janoshik databases, and inspect the product visually.

### Q3: What purity level should I look for?

For research-grade peptides, 98% or above (as measured by HPLC) is the generally accepted standard. Pharmaceutical-grade products exceed 99%. Purity below 95% indicates significant impurities. Be aware that HPLC purity can be inflated by co-eluting isomeric impurities that the standard test cannot separate -- this is a known issue specifically with BPC-157 due to its Asp-Asp sequence motif.

### Q4: Is a COA enough to trust a product?

No. A COA is only as trustworthy as the laboratory that issued it and the chain of custody between the lab and the product. Vendor-supplied COAs may not represent the specific batch you received, may be from the vendor's own lab rather than an independent third party, or may be fabricated. Use the 9-point checklist, verify through the lab's portal if available, and consider independent testing for high-value or health-critical purchases.

### Q5: How should I store my peptides?

Lyophilized (powder): store at -20C or colder for long-term storage; 2-8C is acceptable for near-term use. Keep vials sealed, away from light, and with desiccant. Reconstituted (liquid): refrigerate at 2-8C immediately; use within 4-6 weeks with bacteriostatic water or 7-14 days with sterile water. Never leave reconstituted peptides at room temperature. See Section 5 for complete guidelines.

### Q6: What is bacteriostatic water and why does it matter?

Bacteriostatic water is sterile water that contains 0.9% benzyl alcohol as a preservative. The benzyl alcohol inhibits bacterial growth, making it safe for multi-dose use over approximately 28 days (per USP standards). Without this preservative, bacteria can grow in the solution and metabolize the peptide, causing both degradation and contamination. If you plan to draw multiple doses from one vial, use bacteriostatic water.

### Q7: Is Finnrick testing reliable?

Finnrick provides the largest publicly available peptide quality dataset and is a valuable resource. However, it has acknowledged limitations: it is not ISO-accredited at the organizational level, it contracts testing to third-party labs with documented inter-lab discrepancies, and it operates a vendor revenue model. Use Finnrick data as one data point alongside Janoshik results, community reports, and your own COA evaluation -- not as the sole basis for purchasing decisions.

### Q8: What is the difference between TFA salt, acetate salt, and arginine salt?

These refer to the "counter-ion" attached to the peptide. TFA (trifluoroacetic acid) salt is the default form from manufacturing and can comprise up to 35% of the gross weight, meaning a "5 mg" TFA-salt vial may contain only 3.5-4.0 mg of actual peptide. Acetate salt is lighter, yielding more peptide per milligram of product, and is preferred for injectables. Arginine salt (arginate) is specifically designed for oral delivery -- it shows 12x better acid survival than acetate (93.6% retention at pH 3 versus 7.8% for acetate). The salt form does not determine purity but significantly affects net peptide content and appropriate route of administration.

### Q9: Should I test my peptides?

At minimum, every consumer should perform the free "Tier 0" checks: visual inspection, COA review, and Finnrick database lookup. For peptides worth more than $50 per vial, a basic HPLC test ($7-$200) provides meaningful quality assurance. For injectable peptides where health risk is a factor, or when using a new or unverified vendor, identity confirmation (LC-MS) and endotoxin testing add significant safety information. The cost of testing should be weighed against both the financial value of the product and the health risk of using an unverified product.

### Q10: What changed with peptide regulations in 2026?

Two major developments: First, HHS Secretary Kennedy announced on February 27, 2026, that approximately 14 of 19 Category 2 peptides would be reclassified to Category 1, potentially restoring access through compounding pharmacies. However, no formal FDA rule change has been published as of March 2026. Second, the gray-market research peptide supply chain continued to collapse, with Peptide Sciences (the largest vendor) shutting down on March 6, 2026, following DOJ prosecutions, FDA enforcement, and manufacturer litigation. The market is in a transitional period between the old gray-market model and a potential new regulated compounding pathway.

### Q11: Can I trust telehealth platforms selling peptides?

Telehealth platforms vary widely in quality and legitimacy. Some (like Hims & Hers, Ro) are large publicly traded companies that partner with licensed pharmacies. Others operate with less oversight. The FDA issued warning letters to telehealth platforms marketing compounded GLP-1 products, and Novo Nordisk sued Hims & Hers in February 2026 (later resolved through a collaboration deal in March 2026). Key questions for any telehealth platform: Is the prescribing physician licensed in your state? Is the pharmacy licensed and registered? Can they provide batch-specific testing results?

### Q12: Are oral peptide supplements (capsules) legitimate?

Some companies sell oral BPC-157 capsules as dietary supplements, typically using the arginine salt form. The arginine salt shows significantly better acid survival than acetate (93.6% vs. 7.8% at pH 3), making it the rational form for oral delivery. However, quality verification is even more challenging in the supplement space, as these products fall under DSHEA dietary supplement regulations rather than drug regulations. Importantly, the FDA has not approved BPC-157 for any therapeutic use regardless of delivery form.

### Q13: What about international regulation?

Regulations vary significantly by country. In **Australia**, BPC-157 and several other peptides are Schedule 4 (prescription only) since 2022, with fines exceeding AUD $200,000 for unauthorized importation. In the **UK**, most research peptides remain unscheduled under the Misuse of Drugs Act but must be labeled "for research purposes only." In **Canada**, peptides intended for therapeutic use require Health Canada approval. In the **EU**, member states impose varying restrictions, and the EMA published updated guidelines on synthetic peptide development in December 2025.

### Q14: What happens if I report an adverse event?

You can report adverse events to the FDA's MedWatch program online, by phone, or by mail. Reports go into the FAERS database and are used to identify safety signals. Your report helps protect other consumers and contributes to the evidence base that regulators use to take enforcement action. Healthcare providers can also file reports on your behalf.
