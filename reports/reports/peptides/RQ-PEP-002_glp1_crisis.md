# RQ-PEP-002: The GLP-1 Compounding Crisis

**Research Question:** What are the regulatory, safety, and consumer protection dimensions of the GLP-1 compounding and counterfeiting crisis (2024-2026)?

**Priority:** HIGH -- Immediate consumer safety implications in a $70B+ market

**Date:** 2026-03-15

---

## Executive Summary

The GLP-1 receptor agonist market -- dominated by semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound) -- has become the epicenter of a multi-front pharmaceutical safety crisis. With branded GLP-1 revenue exceeding $33 billion in 2025 and projected at $36 billion in 2026, the enormous demand-supply gap spawned a parallel ecosystem of compounding pharmacies, gray-market peptide sellers, and outright counterfeiters that has now triggered the most aggressive FDA enforcement campaign in a decade.

**Key findings:**

- The FDA issued **50+ warning letters** to compounders (September 2025) and **30 warning letters** to telehealth companies (March 2026) -- a volume exceeding the prior decade's total combined enforcement actions.
- Novo Nordisk's independent testing found compounded semaglutide with **impurities as high as 86%**, dosing ranging from **0.4% to 302%** of labeled strength, and contaminants including oxidized peptide fragments, aggregates, truncated chains, and formaldehyde-related adducts.
- A peer-reviewed JMIR study found online-sold semaglutide with purity of only **7.7-14.37%** (vs. 99% claimed) and endotoxin contamination in **100% of samples**.
- The FDA's FAERS database recorded **900+ adverse events** including **17 deaths** associated with compounded semaglutide/tirzepatide as of December 2024, with **455+ reports** for compounded semaglutide alone by February 2025.
- **38 state attorneys general** petitioned the FDA over counterfeit GLP-1s entering the U.S. from China, Turkey, and India.
- Counterfeit Ozempic pens were seized from the legitimate U.S. drug supply chain in April and December 2025.

## Key Findings

### 1. FDA Enforcement Actions

The FDA's enforcement campaign against the compounded GLP-1 ecosystem has been historically aggressive:

**September 2025 -- 50+ Warning Letters to Compounders:**
- Targeted U.S. and international companies compounding or manufacturing semaglutide and tirzepatide
- Primary violations: false/misleading claims that compounded products are "generic versions" or contain the "same active ingredient" as FDA-approved drugs
- Companies marketing products labeled "research use only" directly to consumers for injection
- cGMP violations, use of APIs from non-FDA-registered sources, incomplete labeling

**March 2026 -- 30 Warning Letters to Telehealth Companies:**
- Companies placing their own brand names on labels, implying they compound the drugs (when they do not)
- Promotional materials making new drug claims and implying FDA approval
- Marketing practices that do not qualify for 503A or 503B exemptions
- FDA Commissioner Makary stated the agency is "paying close attention to misleading claims"

**Import Controls:**
- Import Alert 66-80 ("Green List") established September 2025
- Authorizes border detention of GLP-1 APIs from manufacturers not on the inspected/approved list
- Names of green-listed manufacturers are redacted from public documents
- Targets semaglutide, tirzepatide, liraglutide, exenatide, dulaglutide, and related peptides

### 2. The JMIR Purity Study

**Citation:** "Multifactor Quality and Safety Analysis of Semaglutide Products Sold by Online Sellers Without a Prescription" (JMIR, November 2024)

**Methodology:** Market surveillance, content analysis, and test-purchase evaluation of semaglutide products from online vendors selling without prescriptions.

**Critical Findings:**

| Parameter | Result | Expected |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| **Semaglutide purity** | 7.7% - 14.37% | 99% (as labeled) |
| **Semaglutide content vs. label** | 28.56% - 38.69% excess | Matches label |
| **Endotoxin contamination** | 2.16 - 8.95 EU/mg (100% of samples) | Below USP limits |
| **Peptide-like impurities** | Not detected | -- |
| **Viable microorganisms** | Not detected at time of testing | -- |
| **Visual inspection compliance** | Failed 59-63% of criteria | 100% |

**Interpretation:** Consumers purchasing semaglutide from online sellers received products with roughly **1/7th to 1/13th** the expected purity, with significant excess content (overdose risk) and universal endotoxin contamination. All purchased vials were classified as "probable substandard and falsified products."

### 3. Novo Nordisk's Independent Testing

Novo Nordisk conducted its own testing of compounded semaglutide products, with results disclosed during its February 2026 legal action against Hims & Hers:

**Injectable semaglutide:**
- Impurities as high as **86%**
- One sample contained semaglutide at **302% of labeled strength** (3x intended dose)
- Contaminants detected: oxidized peptide fragments, semaglutide aggregates, truncated peptide chains, formaldehyde-related adducts

**Oral/sublingual semaglutide:**
- Impurities as high as **75%**
- One sublingual tablet contained only **0.4% of labeled semaglutide** (essentially zero active drug)

**Significance:** In many compounded samples, the majority of material in the vial was not intact semaglutide. The range from 0.4% to 302% of labeled strength means patients face dual risks: receiving either a negligible dose (no therapeutic effect) or a dangerous overdose.

### 4. Counterfeit Supply Chain

**Source Countries:**
- **China:** Primary source of counterfeit APIs and "research-grade" peptides sold directly to consumers. The Made In China platform was subject to a Connecticut AG civil investigative demand.
- **Turkey:** Identified by the 38-AG coalition as a key source of counterfeit products repackaged as FDA-approved.
- **India:** Third major source country for counterfeit and substandard GLP-1 APIs.

**Entry Vectors:**
1. **Direct-to-consumer online sales** -- "Research use only" peptides sold via e-commerce, social media (Facebook, Instagram), with implicit or explicit instructions for self-injection
2. **Counterfeit branded products** -- Fake Ozempic pens with authentic-appearing lot numbers entering the legitimate wholesale distribution chain
3. **Unregistered compounding pharmacies** -- Using non-FDA-registered API sources, some listing nonexistent pharmacy addresses on labels
4. **International e-commerce platforms** -- European Medicines Agency flagged illegal GLP-1s on Facebook, e-commerce listings

**Seized Counterfeit Products:**
- **April 2025:** Several hundred counterfeit Ozempic 1mg pens (lot PAR0362, fraudulent serial numbers starting with 51746517)
- **December 2025:** Additional dozens of counterfeit Ozempic 1mg pens (lot PAR1229, identifiable by "EXP/LOT" text placement)
- Neither the FDA nor Novo Nordisk could confirm the contents, quality, or safety of seized products

### 5. Adverse Events and Consumer Harm

**FDA FAERS Data (as of December 31, 2024):**
- **900+ adverse events** associated with compounded semaglutide and tirzepatide
- **17 deaths** reported
- **100+ hospitalizations** (per Novo Nordisk estimates)

**Common adverse events:** Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, constipation -- many requiring emergency medical attention.

**Root causes of harm:**
- **Dosing errors:** Compounded injectables require patients to measure and self-administer doses from multi-dose vials (unlike pre-filled branded pens), leading to over/under-dosing
- **Contamination:** Endotoxins, oxidized fragments, aggregates, and formaldehyde adducts can trigger immune responses including anaphylaxis
- **Wrong substances:** Some products contained repackaged insulin falsely labeled as GLP-1 drugs
- **No active ingredient:** Products with as little as 0.4% of labeled drug provide zero therapeutic benefit, leaving patients' underlying conditions untreated

### 6. Compounding Pharmacy Landscape

**503A vs. 503B Compounders:**

| Feature | 503A (State-Licensed Pharmacies) | 503B (Outsourcing Facilities) |
|---------|----------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Regulation** | State boards of pharmacy | FDA-registered, FDA-inspected |
| **Prescription required** | Yes (patient-specific) | No (can distribute without patient-specific prescriptions) |
| **Scale** | Individual prescriptions | Larger batch production |
| **Semaglutide grace period** | Until Apr 22, 2025 | Until May 22, 2025 |
| **Tirzepatide grace period** | Until Feb 18, 2025 | Until Mar 19, 2025 |
| **Post-grace compounding** | Only with "significant modification" (e.g., allergy-based formulation change) | Same restriction |

**Key Regulatory Principle:** Neither 503A nor 503B facilities may compound an "essentially a copy" of a commercially available FDA-approved drug -- unless that drug is on the FDA shortage list at the time of compounding. With both semaglutide and tirzepatide removed from the shortage list, the legal basis for most GLP-1 compounding has evaporated.

**The Hims & Hers Resolution (March 9, 2026):**
- Novo Nordisk dropped its patent infringement lawsuit
- Hims will sell branded Ozempic (0.5/1/2mg) and Wegovy (injections + tablets) on its platform
- Hims will cease advertising compounded GLP-1s (except limited medically necessary cases)
- Existing compounded-semaglutide patients to be transitioned to FDA-approved products
- HIMS stock surged 46% on the announcement

### Highest-Risk Populations

1. **Uninsured/underinsured patients** seeking cheaper alternatives to branded GLP-1s ($800-1,300/month without insurance)
2. **Cosmetic weight-loss users** without medical oversight, purchasing through social media ads
3. **Patients transitioning off compounded products** who may turn to gray-market sources when compounders lose authorization
4. **International purchasers** ordering from unregulated online pharmacies

### Specific Dangers by Product Type

**"Research-grade" peptides from online sellers:**
- 7-14% purity (JMIR study)
- Universal endotoxin contamination
- No dosing guidance, sterility assurance, or medical oversight
- Highest risk category

**Compounded injectables from telehealth platforms:**
- Impurities up to 86% (Novo testing)
- Dosing from 0.4% to 302% of labeled strength
- Self-administration from multi-dose vials increases dosing error risk
- 455+ FDA adverse event reports

**Counterfeit branded pens:**
- Unknown contents -- could contain anything
- Enter legitimate supply chain, may be dispensed by unsuspecting pharmacies
- Identified by lot/serial number mismatches

## Recommendations

### For Consumers

1. **Only obtain GLP-1 medications via prescription from a licensed healthcare provider, filled at a state-licensed retail pharmacy** that sources from authorized Novo Nordisk or Eli Lilly distributors.
2. **Never purchase semaglutide or tirzepatide labeled "for research use only"** -- this is a legal fiction; these products are marketed for human injection.
3. **Inspect all packaging carefully:** Look for poor print quality, misspellings, unusual label placement, and verify lot/serial numbers against FDA alerts at [FDA MedWatch](https://www.fda.gov/safety/medwatch-fda-safety-information-and-adverse-event-reporting-program).
4. **Know that generic semaglutide does not exist.** Any product marketed as "generic Ozempic" is fraudulent.
5. **If price is a barrier**, explore Novo Nordisk's patient assistance programs, manufacturer coupons, or discuss alternative FDA-approved therapies with a physician. Novo has cut U.S. GLP-1 list prices by up to 70%.
6. **Report suspected counterfeits** to FDA MedWatch (1-800-FDA-1088) and discontinue use immediately.

### For Healthcare Providers

1. **Educate patients** about the risks of compounded and online-sourced GLP-1s, especially the dosing variability (0.4%-302% of labeled strength).
2. **Verify supply chain** for any non-branded GLP-1 products -- confirm 503A/503B compliance and API source registration.
3. **Report adverse events** from compounded or suspected counterfeit products to FDA FAERS.
4. **Assist patient transitions** from compounded to FDA-approved products, leveraging the Hims/Novo partnership model and expanded access programs.

### For Regulators

1. **Expand the Green List program** and make manufacturer names public to increase supply chain transparency.
2. **Coordinate with DHS/CBP** on import interdiction using Import Alert 66-80 as the framework.
3. **Pursue state-level enforcement** against online platforms facilitating direct-to-consumer peptide sales (following the CT AG model).
4. **Mandate third-party testing** for any remaining legally compounded GLP-1 products.
